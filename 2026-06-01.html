⚔️ Breaking — Middle East

Iran Deal Still Unsigned: Trump Reworks the MOU as Tensions Flare

U.S. and Iranian negotiators reached a preliminary memorandum of understanding this week to extend the ongoing ceasefire by 60 days, reopen the Strait of Hormuz, and establish a framework for nuclear talks — but the deal remains in limbo. President Trump still has not signed off, and sources say he personally edited the text, toughening language around Iran's enriched uranium stockpile and shipping access. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent made clear there will be no sanctions relief until Iran surrenders its highly enriched uranium.

The situation on the ground remains volatile. Late Friday, U.S. forces launched what the Pentagon called "defensive strikes" in southern Iran; Tehran responded by targeting Kuwait with ballistic missiles. Israel separately expanded operations in southern Lebanon, warning citizens of continued fire toward the north. As June opens, the deal's fate rests entirely with a single signature from the Oval Office — and neither side considers it final.

⚖️ Politics

Powell Stays on as Fed Governor After DOJ Probe Extends His Stay

Jerome Powell's tenure as chair ended when Kevin Warsh was sworn in on May 22, but Powell remains a rank-and-file Fed governor through 2028 due to an ongoing Justice Department investigation. He'll be in the room when Warsh chairs his first FOMC meeting on June 16–17 — a dynamic no Fed chair has faced in over 75 years.

🌐 Geopolitics

Strait of Hormuz Remains the Linchpin of Any Iran Deal

Iran's state media claimed the draft MOU pledges to reopen the Strait to pre-war commercial traffic, but the White House dismissed an earlier report of that detail as a "complete fabrication." Middle East analysts warn that regardless of any agreement's text, Iran now effectively controls the Strait and will for the foreseeable future — a reality that shapes the energy market calculus.

🏛️ Washington

Iran's Highly Enriched Uranium: The Sticking Point

Iran holds 440.9 kilograms of uranium enriched to 60% purity — a short technical step from weapons-grade 90%. Disposing of that stockpile is the first item on the negotiating agenda if a deal is struck. Trump posted from the Situation Room that the "nuclear dust" must either be turned over to the U.S. or destroyed. No resolution on that point has been reported.

₿ Bitcoin

Federal Reserve & Macro
Warsh & the Rate War
🏦 Fed Chair Kevin Warsh

Sent to Cut, Warsh May Have to Hike: The Paradox of the 17th Fed Chair

Kevin Warsh was sworn in as the 17th Federal Reserve Chair on May 22 — the first White House swearing-in for a Fed chair since Alan Greenspan in 1987. He inherits a central bank with inflation at its highest in nearly three years, a bitterly divided FOMC that produced four dissents at its most recent meeting, and a president who wants lower rates. The market has given him no room to maneuver: a 97% probability that rates hold at the June 16–17 meeting, and a 40% chance of a hike by December.

Warsh's stated goals are iconoclastic: abandon the Fed's dot plot (forward guidance), shrink the balance sheet aggressively, and refuse to commit to any political timetable on rates. Market veteran Ed Yardeni has argued Warsh may need to actually raise rates to establish credibility with bond vigilantes — or risk Treasury yields surging further. PCE inflation at 3.8% year-over-year makes any dovish move nearly impossible to defend. His first FOMC press conference on June 17 will define his chairmanship more than the rate decision itself.

"Kevin Warsh will have a hard time convincing anyone to cut rates any time soon."

— Heather Long, Chief Economist, Navy Federal Credit Union

Housing Market
🏠 Mortgage Rates

30-Year Fixed Holds at 6.53% — And Analysts Don't See It Moving Much

The 30-year fixed mortgage rate closed May at 6.53% per Freddie Mac's weekly survey, up from 5.98% in late February as the Iran conflict and rising inflation pushed rates higher. Fannie Mae projects rates stay between 6.1% and 6.3% for the remainder of 2026. The Mortgage Bankers Association echoes that range, noting the war in the Middle East is a key upside risk. For now, every fraction of a percent matters: Zillow estimates a one-percentage-point drop in rates would unlock 5.5 million new potential buyers.

📊 Spring Market

Listings Finally Outpace Buyers — A Spring Season Unlike Any Other

Existing home sales edged up just 0.2% in April to a 4.02 million annual pace, while unsold inventory rose to 1.47 million units — 4.4 months of supply. That is the first time this cycle that listings have meaningfully outpaced sales, and economists are calling the spring season "weird." Fannie Mae projects home prices will rise 3.2% in 2026 and 1.9% in 2027 — sluggish by pandemic-era standards but stable. Case-Shiller's year-over-year reading of +0.7% in the most recent data reflects how much the market has cooled.

😴 Sleep Science

The Goldilocks Window: 6.4 to 7.8 Hours Is the Sweet Spot for Healthy Aging

A sweeping new study published last week adds nuance to the growing body of sleep science: getting too much sleep appears to be nearly as harmful as too little. Researchers found that consistently sleeping more than 7.8 hours per night is associated with accelerated aging across nearly every organ system — a finding that challenges the "more is better" intuition many people hold about rest. The ideal window, based on the data: 6.4 to 7.8 hours. Earlier Oregon Health & Science University research from January found insufficient sleep (under 7 hours) outweighed even diet and exercise as a predictor of lifespan — suggesting that sleep quality belongs at the very top of any serious wellness hierarchy.

🧠 Neurowellness

Microplastics Are Everywhere in the Body. 2026 Is the Year We Start Acting on It.

The Global Wellness Summit's 2026 trend report names microplastics as "a real threat" — distinguishing it from decades of wellness industry "detox" rhetoric. The report notes that microplastics are now detectable throughout the human body and are increasingly linked to serious health conditions. Researchers and public health authorities are moving from awareness to action this year, pushing for both regulatory responses and consumer-facing interventions. Separately, regulators the nervous system is wellness' newest frontier, with consumer neurotechnology expanding rapidly. If stress is the chronic illness of the 2020s, regulating the fight-or-flight response is shaping up to be its antidote.
