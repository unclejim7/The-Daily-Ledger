Geopolitics & Markets

Qatari Mediators Race to Tehran as Trump Declares Hormuz Deal "Scheduled" for His Birthday

Qatari negotiators flew into Tehran in the early hours of June 14 in a last push to lock down a memorandum of understanding meant to end the months-long U.S.-Israel war with Iran and reopen the Strait of Hormuz to global shipping. President Trump declared Saturday that the agreement is "scheduled to get signed tomorrow" — June 14, his 80th birthday — and said the strait would be "OPEN TO ALL" immediately upon signing. Tehran has not matched that timeline: Iran's Foreign Ministry spokesperson said over the weekend that a signing "will not be tomorrow," and Iranian state media framed Trump's birthday timing as a bid for a personal publicity moment.

Logistics remain unsettled, with both a remote signing and a later in-person ceremony floated as options. The diplomatic push comes against a backdrop of continued violence in the broader theater: Israeli forces struck Hezbollah infrastructure in Beirut's southern suburbs early Sunday, and Hezbollah claimed a drone strike on Israeli positions in southern Lebanon overnight. Inside Iran, IRGC-linked media continues to characterize Trump's announced timeline as a "test" of Iranian negotiators rather than a settled outcome.

Markets have nonetheless leaned into optimism: oil prices tumbled more than 3% on hopes that a deal — even an imperfect one — would ease the months-long blockade pressure that has rattled energy and shipping markets since February. The S&P 500 and Dow both posted their best weekly gains in months on the back of the de-escalation headlines, with the rally extending into Friday's session on continued optimism.

Ceasefire Rally

Stocks Surge as Trump Cancels Planned Iran Strikes

Wall Street staged its sharpest rally in weeks after President Trump said he had called off planned strikes against Iran and signaled a peace deal was close, reversing days of war-driven selling. The Dow jumped more than 900 points, the S&P 500 climbed over 1.7%, and the Nasdaq led gains with a 2.5% surge as crude oil tumbled on relief that the Hormuz blockade might soon ease. Friday's session extended the calm, with the Dow advancing another 0.7% to close above 51,200 on continued optimism about the U.S.-Iran framework and a strong follow-through from SpaceX's blockbuster IPO debut. Macroeconomics & The Federal Reserve

FOMC Preview — June 16-17

Markets All But Certain of a Hold as Warsh Faces First Meeting Under Pressure

The Federal Open Market Committee meets Tuesday and Wednesday for its first decision under new Chair Kevin Warsh, who took over on May 15. Prediction markets put the odds of no change to the federal funds rate — currently 3.50%-3.75% — at roughly 89-99%, the firmest consensus of the year. The case for a hold rests on a familiar tension: headline CPI running around 4.2% year-over-year, well above the Fed's 2% target, against a labor market that remains resilient near 4.3% unemployment. April's FOMC minutes show a committee already divided, with several members pushing for two-sided language on future moves rather than a clear easing bias. Energy-driven inflation pressure tied to the Iran conflict has been repeatedly cited as a complicating factor for any near-term cut, and some traders are now pricing modest odds of a hike later this year if inflation data don't cooperate. Warsh's press conference Wednesday afternoon will be parsed closely for his first real signal on direction.

Labor Market

Unemployment Holds Near 4.3%-4.4% Amid Resilient Hiring

The labor market continues to run hotter than the Fed would like for a clean disinflation story, with unemployment near 4.3%-4.4% and recent payroll reports topping expectations. That resilience, paired with energy-driven inflation pressure, is the core tension Chair Warsh inherits heading into Tuesday's meeting.

Real Estate & Mortgage Rates

Rates Ease Into the Weekend

30-Year Fixed Slips to 6.35% as Buyers Get a Bit of Breathing Room

The average 30-year fixed mortgage rate slipped to 6.35% APR as of Sunday morning, down a few basis points from Saturday and nine basis points below where it stood a week ago — though still elevated versus the lows seen earlier this spring. The 15-year fixed rose modestly to 5.99% APR, while the 5-year ARM held steady near 6.47%. Freddie Mac's weekly survey through June 11 pegged the 30-year average at 6.52%, a meaningful 32 basis points below this time last year. The Mortgage Bankers Association expects 30-year rates to hover between 6.4% and 6.5% through the rest of 2026, while Fannie Mae's house view sits closer to 6.3%. The persistent driver behind the recent upward pressure: sticky inflation tied to energy costs from the Iran conflict, plus heavy Treasury issuance pushing yields — and mortgage rates — higher in lockstep. Freddie Mac's chief economist Sam Khater has noted that buyers appear to be "looking past short-term rate fluctuations and actively entering the market," pushing existing home sales to a five-month high.

Armstrong Economics — ECM & War Cycle

Cycles & Geopolitical Brushfires

Armstrong: Sovereign Debt Strains and "Brushfire Wars" Point Hard Toward Gold

Martin Armstrong's recent commentary continues to frame the current moment through his Economic Confidence Model, which is on track to peak at its downcycle midpoint sometime in 2026 before beginning a multi-year decline. Armstrong has been warning of an escalating pattern of geopolitical "brushfire" conflicts — with Iran's strategy increasingly read as an attempt to trigger broader energy and banking disruption — layered on top of what he sees as a building sovereign debt crisis among major economies. His long-standing view remains that this combination of currency instability, war risk, and government overreach favors hard assets, with gold continuing to serve as the primary hedge in his framework. Gold's push above $4,200 this past week, up roughly 3% on the day amid the Iran headlines, fits the pattern Armstrong has flagged repeatedly: capital flight to metal during acute geopolitical stress. Readers following the ECM should watch how bond markets react around the FOMC decision this week, which Armstrong-aligned analysts view as a potential confirmation point for the next phase of the cycle.

Precision Wellness: Wearables and At-Home Diagnostics Take Center Stage

This month's health and wellness landscape is dominated by a shift toward data-driven, personalized care. New high-fidelity sensor wearables and AI-powered sleep recovery systems are pushing toward clinical-grade insight for everyday consumers, while portable diagnostic tools — including saliva-based cancer screening developed at the University of Hong Kong — hint at a future where early detection happens outside the doctor's office. At the same time, "grab-and-go" wellness formats remain popular: functional matcha drinks, dissolvable supplement strips, and creatine-and-electrolyte boosters are reshaping how people fit nutrition into busy days.

Strength Training and Longevity Lead 2026 Wellness Priorities

A widely cited annual wellness survey found 82% of respondents plan to focus more on overall health this year, with strength training (46.5%) now outranking weight loss as the top fitness goal. Longevity was cited by roughly a third of respondents as their leading motivation, and over a third say they already use AI tools — for workout programming and nutrition tracking — regularly or occasionally.
