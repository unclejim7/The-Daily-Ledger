Breaking · Federal Reserve

Kevin Warsh's Fed Era Begins: First FOMC Meeting Opens Today as Inflation Runs Hot and Trump Says He "Loved" It

Kevin Warsh gavels in his first Federal Open Market Committee meeting today, a two-day session running through Wednesday that will produce his debut policy decision, an updated Summary of Economic Projections and dot plot, and his first post-meeting press conference at 2:30 PM Eastern on Wednesday. Warsh was sworn in as the Fed's 17th chair on May 22 after a 54-45 Senate confirmation vote, in a White House ceremony where Supreme Court Justice Clarence Thomas administered the oath. Wall Street's near-universal expectation is that the committee holds the federal funds rate at 3.50%–3.75%, with the more interesting question being whether the policy statement drops its long-standing "easing bias" language in favor of a neutral stance.

Warsh inherits a trickier inflation picture than markets had hoped for a few months ago. May's consumer price index ran at 4.2% year-over-year, with producer prices up an even hotter 6.5%, much of it traced to the energy-price spike from the now-pausing Iran war. Complicating matters politically, President Trump said on June 9 that he "loved the inflation" — widely read by Fed-watchers as giving Warsh cover to hold rates rather than cut, even as Trump has separately pushed for lower rates elsewhere. Bank of America economist Ethan Harris noted the committee's collective tone has turned "more hawkish" heading into this week, and that Wednesday's new economic projections will likely show both higher inflation and a slightly higher funds-rate path than March's forecast.

That leaves Warsh squeezed from two directions at once: a bond market pricing in some chance rates may need to rise, and a president publicly demanding they fall. Warsh has said he favors "messier meetings" with room for "a good family fight" among the twelve voting members, and a Fed that communicates less aggressively than under predecessor Jerome Powell — who has agreed to remain on the board through the transition. None of that changes Wednesday's headline outcome, which virtually every economist expects to be no change at all; the real story will be what Warsh says about it, and how markets read a new chair finding his footing in real time.

The backdrop is calmer than it might have been. Monday's stocks rallied to fresh records — the S&P 500 up 1.65% to 7,554.29, the Dow up 0.92% to 51,671.03, the Nasdaq surging 3.07% to 26,683.94 — on news that the U.S. and Iran had reached a peace agreement, with a formal signing ceremony set for Friday in Switzerland. Tuesday's futures sat flat to slightly lower as traders shifted their attention from Hormuz back to the Eccles Building.

Federal Reserve

FOMC Day One · June 16

This Is Also a Projections Meeting — Watch the Dot Plot, Not Just the Statement

June's meeting is one of the Fed's quarterly "projections" sessions, meaning Wednesday brings a fresh Summary of Economic Projections alongside the rate decision — updated GDP, unemployment, and inflation forecasts plus the closely watched dot plot showing each official's own rate-path guess. JPMorgan Wealth Management's Phil Camporeale expects "an explicit move away from a bias toward easing to a neutral stance," which would be the most concrete signal of how Warsh intends to run the committee differently than Powell did, even before he says a word in Wednesday's press conference.

Axios reports Warsh faces "a restive wing of monetary hawks" on the committee and has offered almost no public comment on policy since taking office, leaving investors with limited material to work from heading into his debut. That silence, paired with his stated preference for messier, more openly contested meetings, makes Wednesday as much a test of communication style as of monetary policy.

Geopolitics & Current Events

Iran Deal · Pre-Implementation Week

Doha Meetings This Week Lay Groundwork for Friday's Switzerland Signing

With the framework agreement announced Sunday, mediators are running separate preparatory meetings with the U.S. and Iranian delegations in Doha this week to set up Friday's formal signing ceremony in Switzerland and the technical talks that follow. The deal calls for an immediate and permanent end to military operations, including in Lebanon, and a 60-day window for follow-up negotiations on the harder unresolved issues — Iran's nuclear program, sanctions relief, and the precise terms governing freedom of navigation through the Strait of Hormuz.

Israel's Position

Netanyahu Seeks Trump Meeting; Israel Calls Itself a Non-Party to the Deal

Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, who was not party to the U.S.-Iran negotiations, is reportedly seeking a meeting with Trump — potentially in Washington — to discuss the agreement. Netanyahu has said he and Trump remain in "full agreement" that Iran must not obtain nuclear weapons, even as Israel's government has described the deal in its current form as a deep disappointment.

International Reaction

EU, France, and U.K. Welcome the Framework — With Conditions

European Union President António Costa called the agreement a step toward "lasting peace" and said the bloc stands ready to help, while French President Emmanuel Macron described unrestricted, toll-free maritime traffic through Hormuz as "an essential condition for regional stability and the global economy." Both leaders' statements stopped short of declaring the conflict fully resolved, echoing the same wait-and-see posture markets have adopted after two prior ceasefires collapsed within days.

Real Estate & Mortgage Rates

NAR Existing-Home Sales · May 2026

Home Sales Hit Fastest Pace Since December as Rates Ease Off Their Highs

Existing-home sales rose 3.2% in May to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 4.17 million units, their strongest pace since December, according to the National Association of Realtors. The median sales price climbed to a record $429,300, up 1.3% from a year ago. NAR chief economist Lawrence Yun credited improving affordability for the rebound, noting buyers are "looking past short-term rate fluctuations and actively entering the market" even with mortgage rates still well above pre-pandemic norms.

Daily rate trackers showed modest further easing Tuesday morning: NerdWallet put the average 30-year fixed rate at 6.32% APR, down two basis points, while Freddie Mac's most recent weekly survey (week of June 11) had the 30-year averaging 6.52%, down from 6.84% a year ago. Over 80% of current homeowners carry mortgages below 6%, the lock-in effect that continues to constrain how much existing inventory reaches the market even as new listings tick higher.

Armstrong Economics

Sovereign Debt & Gold

Armstrong: Geopolitical "Brushfires" and a Sovereign Debt Crisis Keep Gold as the Ultimate Hedge

Martin Armstrong's capital-flow model continues to flag escalating sovereign debt stress alongside scattered geopolitical flare-ups — the same kind of pattern, he says, his system picked up ahead of Russia's actions in Ukraine and recent Middle East tensions. In recent commentary, Armstrong reiterated a bullish multi-year gold outlook, with resistance eyed near $8,500 an ounce and a further target near $10,000, alongside a silver range of roughly $165 to $200. He's framed the case less as a pure war hedge and more as a signal of institutional capital fleeing confidence in sovereign debt generally — a thesis Monday's odd combination of rallying stocks and rallying gold did nothing to disprove.

Armstrong's broader argument remains that capital controls, not war itself, are the bigger long-run risk: Spain's recent cap on withdrawals without government approval is the kind of policy he's pointed to as a precursor to digital-currency-driven controls elsewhere. With Warsh's debut FOMC meeting landing the same week as the Iran deal's formal signing, Armstrong's Economic Confidence Model continues to flag the back half of 2026 as a pivotal stretch for testing whether this is a genuine de-escalation or another "temporary lull" before the next leg of his war cycle.

Health, Wellness & Fitness

The Strength Training "Sweet Spot": 90 to 120 Minutes a Week

A study tracking more than 147,000 people found that just 90 to 120 minutes of strength training per week was associated with some of the largest long-term longevity benefits observed — reinforcing a broader shift in fitness guidance away from pure cardio volume and toward resistance work as a foundational habit, not an optional add-on.

Stanford Researchers Regrow Lost Cartilage, Reverse Arthritis in Mice

A new treatment that blocks an aging-related protein restored lost cartilage in older mice and helped prevent arthritis from developing after knee injuries, with human cartilage samples showing similar encouraging signs in early lab work. It's a long way from a clinical therapy, but it's one of the more concrete steps yet toward addressing joint degeneration at a cellular level rather than just managing its symptoms.

GLP-1 Users Are Moving Less, Fitbit Data Suggests

Researchers analyzing Fitbit activity data found that daily step counts and overall exercise levels dropped among people taking popular weight-loss drugs like Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro, and Zepbound after they started the medication — a reminder that the scale moving in the right direction doesn't automatically mean activity habits are following along, and that pairing GLP-1 therapy with intentional movement may matter more than it gets credit for.

Editor's Dispatch

New Fed chair, same old tension: a bond market that thinks rates might need to go up, a president who wants them to come down, and a chair who's promised messier meetings instead of papering over the disagreement. None of that changes tomorrow's outcome — everyone expects a hold — but the dot plot and the press conference will tell us more about how Warsh actually runs this committee than anything he's said publicly since May. Throw in Doha pre-talks ahead of Friday's Switzerland signing, a Cubs-Rockies rematch tonight, and a real severe weather risk Wednesday afternoon, and there's a lot riding on the next 48 hours that has nothing to do with each other and everything to do with whether this week ends calmer than it started.

Random Fact

The Federal Reserve isn't a single bank but a system of twelve regional reserve banks, a structure baked into the 1913 Federal Reserve Act partly so that no single region or faction could dominate the nation's monetary policy — a design choice today's new chair inherits along with the gavel.
