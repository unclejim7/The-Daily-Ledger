⚔️ Iran — Top Story

Negotiators Strike a 60-Day Deal — Now It's Trump's Call

U.S. and Iranian negotiators have agreed on a 60-day memorandum of understanding to extend the ceasefire and launch nuclear talks — but the deal remains in limbo pending President Trump's final approval. The president met with advisers in the White House Situation Room on Friday and posted his red lines on Truth Social: Iran must "never have a Nuclear Weapon or Bomb" and the Strait of Hormuz must be "immediately open, no tolls, for unrestricted shipping traffic." He called the moment his opportunity to make a "final determination."

"No money will be exchanged, until further notice. Other items, of far less importance, have been agreed to."

— President Trump, via Truth Social, May 29

What the deal would entail: Shipping through the Strait of Hormuz would be unrestricted; Iran would remove mines within 30 days; the U.S. naval blockade would lift proportionally as commercial shipping resumes. Key sticking points remain — Iran insists on its right to domestic uranium enrichment, while Trump demands the entire nuclear program be dismantled. Markets finished the week at all-time highs in part on ceasefire optimism, with oil prices falling and tech stocks surging. The resolution of this conflict — or its failure — will set the tone for global markets through the summer.

Lebanon: Renewed Israeli Strikes on Beirut

Macroeconomics
📉 Consumer Economy

PCE Running Hot at 3.8% — Savings Rate Near 2022 Lows

April PCE came in at 3.8% year-over-year, a three-year high. Core PCE at 3.3%. Real consumer spending rose only 0.1% while real disposable income fell 1.4% year-over-year. The personal saving rate has dropped to near its lowest level since 2022 — consumers are spending, but they're dipping into savings to do it. Cocoa prices, meanwhile, are down more than 50% on the year, a bright spot for some discretionary categories even as overall inflation remains elevated.

Dimon Warns of Coming "Credit Recession"

JPMorgan CEO Jamie Dimon offered a cautious message this week, warning vaguely that credit recessions "when we have one" can be severe and are often underestimated. While he pointed to no specific current signal, the comment came as high-yield spreads and private credit conditions are receiving increased scrutiny. The War in Iran and elevated oil prices have complicated economic modeling for the remainder of 2026.

Federal Reserve
🏛️ Chair Warsh — New Era Begins

Markets Pricing In Minimal Cuts; Warsh Faces an Inflation Bind

Kevin Warsh was confirmed by the Senate 54–45 on May 13 — the most divisive Fed confirmation in history — and officially took the helm on May 15. The new chair was originally seen as potentially dovish (some analysts projected 100 bps in cuts by October), but with PCE running at 3.8% and the Iran conflict keeping oil elevated, rate cuts in 2026 look increasingly unlikely. Markets are currently pricing in only about 40 basis points in cuts for the rest of the year, versus earlier hopes for 100 bps.

Warsh has made no promises on rate cuts despite being picked by a president who wants lower rates. The Fed held at 3.50–3.75% at the April 29 meeting in an unusual 8-4 vote. The June FOMC meeting will be Warsh's first major test. His priorities — tighter inflation discipline, streamlined communication, and active balance sheet reduction — represent a meaningful shift from the Powell era.

Tax & Accounting
📋 Portfolio Strategy

Inflation Above 3.5%? Time to Revisit Your 60/40

With prices across the U.S. up more than 25% since 2020 and broad bond holdings returning only about 6% in that span, Chase strategists and others are warning that the classic 60/40 stock-bond portfolio may not absorb risk the way it once did. When inflation stays volatile, stocks and bonds can fall simultaneously. The recommendation: consider real assets — commodities, infrastructure, natural resource equities, and global real estate — that have historically shown a positive correlation to inflation.

🏢 Corporate

Exxon Mobil Votes to Move Incorporation to Texas

Exxon Mobil shareholders voted approximately 71% in favor of moving the company's state of incorporation from New Jersey to Texas. The move is largely symbolic for day-to-day operations but reflects a broader trend of major energy corporations aligning their legal home with business-friendly Texas regulations. The vote was confirmed at this week's annual meeting. Exxon's stock has been under pressure this month as oil prices retreated on ceasefire optimism.

Real Estate
🏠 Housing Market

Rates Inch Lower, But the Spring Season Remains "Weird"

The 30-year fixed mortgage rate sits at 6.56% today (Bankrate), down slightly from last week's 6.59% (Zillow/US News). Freddie Mac's Thursday weekly survey put the benchmark at 6.53%, up from 6.51% the prior week. Fannie Mae projects rates will stay near 6.3% through 2026, while NAHB is more optimistic, projecting a drop below 6% by year-end — though that outlook was made before Iran-driven inflation pressures returned.

The housing market picture: National home prices are up only 0.7% year-over-year (Case-Shiller), sluggish by historical standards. Pending home sales have increased three consecutive months, suggesting latent demand. Texas and Florida have shifted to buyer's markets, while parts of the Midwest and Northeast — including the Chicago area — remain seller's markets. The spring buying season is described as "weird" — listings outpaced sales in April for the first time this cycle, but buyers haven't fully materialized to take advantage.

Health & Wellness

Stanford: Why the Aging Brain Slows Down — The Protein Jam Theory

Scientists at Stanford, studying the ultra-short-lived turquoise killifish, may have uncovered a hidden reason human brains decline with age: the cellular machinery responsible for building proteins begins to jam. The disruption in protein synthesis — not just neuron loss — could be a primary driver of cognitive decline. Researchers hope the finding opens new therapeutic targets for age-related neurodegeneration.

Silent Crisis: Kidney Disease Now Affects 800 Million Worldwide

A sweeping global study published this week found that chronic kidney disease now affects nearly 800 million people and has become one of the world's leading causes of death. The condition is often silent in its early stages, making it a major undetected contributor to cardiovascular disease. Researchers are calling for expanded routine screening, particularly for people with diabetes and hypertension — the primary risk factors.

Edibles + Alcohol = Worse Than Either Alone Behind the Wheel

Johns Hopkins researchers found that combining cannabis edibles and alcohol creates significantly greater driving impairment than either substance used alone — and critically, standard field sobriety tests frequently failed to detect the cannabis component. The findings have implications for roadside enforcement and public safety messaging, particularly as recreational cannabis remains widely available.

B12 & Folate Deficiencies Linked to Chronic Fatigue — Even in Otherwise Healthy Adults

Japanese researchers found that low levels of vitamin B12 and folate may quietly contribute to persistent fatigue and low motivation, even in people who appear otherwise healthy and are getting adequate sleep. The study suggests these deficiencies are underdiagnosed and that supplementation — or simply improving diet with leafy greens, legumes, and fortified foods — could offer meaningful energy benefits without pharmaceutical intervention.

✍️ Editor's Dispatch

May closes with a paradox: the best month for stocks in years, and the worst time to be certain about anything. The Nasdaq's 8% surge, the S&P's ninth straight winning week, Dow 51,000 — on paper, it looks like blue skies. But underneath: PCE at a three-year high, a new Fed chair who can't yet cut without credibility, a war ceasefire that still needs one signature, and treasury yields hovering just under 5% on the long end.

The market has decided to believe the deal gets done, the missiles stop flying, the oil comes back online, and Warsh threads the needle. That's a lot of faith to price in on a Friday afternoon. This weekend, the question hanging over every trading desk isn't what happened in May — it's whether June can sustain the story. Trump's "final determination" on Iran may come as early as this weekend. Read this edition, then check the wire.
