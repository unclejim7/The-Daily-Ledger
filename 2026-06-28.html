Sunny and 90°F Today — The Season’s First Serious Heat Wave Is Here and It Runs All Week

Sunday opens the week with full sunshine and a high of 90°F in Hoffman Estates — warm, but still manageable compared to what is coming. The heat builds steadily from here: Monday reaches 94°F, then Tuesday through Thursday sees highs of 94–95°F with heat index values forecast to exceed 105°F during peak afternoon hours. The National Weather Service has designated this a Level 3 of 3 high-risk heat event for the Chicago metro area and northwest suburbs through the Fourth of July holiday weekend. A slight chance of showers and thunderstorms arrives Wednesday evening before the pattern briefly moderates toward the end of next week. For Hoffman Estates, Schaumburg, and Streamwood residents: limit outdoor exertion to before 10 AM or after 6 PM Tuesday through Thursday. Hydrate aggressively before you feel thirsty. Cook County and Kane County are opening cooling centers Monday. Check on neighbors who are elderly or without reliable air conditioning — heat at this level is genuinely dangerous, and the overnight lows in the mid-70s will prevent meaningful cooling between days.

Geopolitics · Military · Sunday Edition

The Ceasefire Cracks: U.S. Strikes Iran Again After Drone Hits Hormuz Tanker — IRGC Retaliates, Bahrain Reports Attacks, and the 60-Day MOU Enters Its Most Dangerous Weekend

The 60-day memorandum of understanding between the United States and Iran — signed just eleven days ago at Versailles — is under its most severe test yet. A sequence of events unfolded rapidly Friday and Saturday: a Panamanian-flagged oil tanker identified by maritime security firm Vanguard Tech as the KIKU was struck by an unidentified projectile in the Strait of Hormuz, sustaining damage to its bridge while carrying more than two million barrels of crude oil. All crew were reported safe and no environmental damage was confirmed, per the UK Maritime Trade Operations monitoring body. The United States military responded by striking what CENTCOM described as Iranian military surveillance infrastructure, communication systems, air defense sites, drone storage facilities, and minelayer capabilities in the southern coastal region near Sirik — marking the second straight night of U.S. strikes on Iranian territory.

Iran’s IRGC responded by claiming it had struck U.S.-linked targets in the region without identifying them specifically. Most dramatically, Bahrain’s Foreign Ministry disclosed that Iranian drones had targeted the Gulf kingdom at dawn Saturday, calling the attacks a “flagrant violation of Bahrain’s sovereignty.” Iran in turn accused the U.S. of violating the MOU framework with its Thursday strikes, with the Iranian Foreign Ministry calling the attacks a “blatant violation” of the accord. The cycle of accusation and counter-accusation that has defined this conflict since February has reasserted itself within a fortnight of the formal signing ceremony at Versailles.

Vice President Vance posted on X that Iran “signed a ceasefire agreement” — implicitly warning that any further escalation carries consequences. The situation arriving into Sunday morning: the MOU is nominally intact and neither side has declared it void, commercial shipping through Hormuz continues at reduced but improving volumes, and oil prices remain at $69 rather than $97. But the pattern of Friday night through Saturday morning — U.S. strike, IRGC retaliation, Gulf state attacks — is precisely the kind of incident cascade that Armstrong Economics’ ECM War Cycle models as characteristic of 2026’s Panic Cycle phase.

Random Fact

The Strait of Hormuz, the maritime chokepoint at the center of the 2026 Iran war, is only 21 miles wide at its narrowest point — roughly the distance from Chicago’s Loop to Hoffman Estates. Yet through that 21-mile gap flows approximately 20% of global petroleum and 20% of the world’s liquefied natural gas. Iran’s closure of the strait in early March 2026 triggered what the International Energy Agency characterized as the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market.

Word of the Day
memorandum

(noun, from Latin: memorandum, “a thing to be remembered”) — in diplomatic usage, a written record of a formal agreement or understanding between parties, typically less legally binding than a treaty but more than an informal communiqué. The US-Iran memorandum of understanding signed June 17 at Versailles is technically a framework for negotiation rather than a final agreement — which is why both sides can simultaneously claim it is being upheld and violated. A memorandum, by definition, is what both parties agree to remember. What they choose to forget is the larger story.
