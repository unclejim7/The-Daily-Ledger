Federal Reserve · Markets · Wednesday Edition

Warsh Makes His Global Debut in Sintra as Bitcoin Slides Below $58,500 to Cap Wall Street's Best Quarter Since 2020

Federal Reserve Chair Kevin Warsh stepped onto the international stage for the first time Wednesday, joining ECB President Christine Lagarde, Bank of England Governor Andrew Bailey, and Bank of Canada Governor Tiff Macklem for a moderated panel at the ECB's annual Forum on Central Banking in Sintra, Portugal. Roughly six weeks into a four-year term that runs through 2030, Warsh stuck to the approach that has defined his brief tenure so far: he declined to signal what the Fed will do at its July 28–29 meeting, reiterating his effort to steer as far as possible from traditional "forward guidance." "There's a lot of late breaking news on a series of these things, and we get into that room and shut the door, we're going to have the good debate," Warsh told the panel's moderator, CNBC's Sara Eisen. "But I don't have much more for you than that." Yardeni Research analysts said ahead of the appearance that they "were surprised by Warsh's hawkishness" following his June 17 press conference, where he pledged to hit the Fed's 2% inflation target and prompted investors to price in rate-hike odds as early as September.

The Sintra debut came a day after Wall Street closed the books on a remarkable second quarter. The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 136 points (+0.26%) Tuesday to a second consecutive record close at 52,319.20, up 8.85% year-to-date. The S&P 500 gained 0.79% to 7,449.36 and the Nasdaq Composite jumped 1.52% to 26,213.72, both notching their best quarterly performance since 2020 — the S&P up roughly 14% and the Nasdaq near 20% for Q2 — powered by a furious chip-stock rally that saw Nvidia, AMD, Intel, Sandisk, and Marvell all post strong Tuesday gains. Wednesday's premarket was comparatively subdued, with futures little changed as investors digested the quarter and turned to Thursday's June jobs report, moved up a day because markets are closed Friday for the July 4th holiday. Tuesday's ADP private payrolls report came in soft at 98,000 jobs added, below the 110,000 forecast and down from May's 122,000, even as May JOLTS data released the same day showed job openings climbing to a two-year high — a mixed signal that leaves Thursday's official print as the week's most important data point.

Geopolitically, the U.S. and Iran pushed forward on de-escalation. Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner met Wednesday with Qatar's prime minister and foreign minister, Sheikh Mohammed bin Abdulrahman bin Jassim Al-Thani, in Doha, with a senior U.S. official telling Bloomberg the talks were "positive" and that progress is being made on technical implementation of the 14-point Islamabad memorandum. The session followed a rocky stretch: a weekend exchange of fire near the Strait of Hormuz that both sides have since agreed to "stand down" from, and Vice President JD Vance's Tuesday comment that the U.S. has "accomplished the core mission" of preventing Iran from obtaining a nuclear weapon. Iranian officials have been more circumspect, with senior negotiator Kazem Gharibabadi declining to confirm the Doha session even as Tehran continued sanctions-relief mechanics, including President Pezeshkian's announcement that $6 billion in frozen Iranian assets held in Qatar would be returned.

Bitcoin did not share in the quarter-end cheer. The largest cryptocurrency slid to roughly $58,300 Wednesday morning, extending a decline that has now pulled it more than 20% lower for June alone — its steepest monthly drop on record for spot Bitcoin ETFs, which shed a combined $4.5 billion in June, the worst month since the products launched in January 2024. The Fear & Greed Index sits at 12, deep in Extreme Fear, and the RSI reading of roughly 30 signals oversold conditions with little sign yet of a floor.

Q2 Wrap

Best Quarter Since 2020 — S&P Up ~14%, Nasdaq ~20% as Chip Stocks Lead the Comeback

Despite an active Iran war, an AI-valuation scare, and a Fed chair transition, the second quarter delivered Wall Street's strongest performance in six years. The S&P 500 gained roughly 14% and the Nasdaq nearly 20%, with the Dow posting its best quarter since 2022 and closing above 52,000 for the first time. Breadth improved along the way — 64% of S&P 500 stocks now trade above their 50-day moving average, up from just 50% a month ago, suggesting the rally is broadening beyond a handful of chip and AI names.

SpaceX Set to Join Nasdaq-100 July 7 — JPMorgan Sees $4.3 Billion in Passive Inflows

SpaceX (SPCX) shares fell 1.74% to $161.34 in Wednesday premarket trading after Monday's 7.2% surge on news the rocket and AI company will be added to the Nasdaq-100 before the market opens July 7. Labor Market

ADP Payrolls Miss at 98,000 as JOLTS Job Openings Hit a Two-Year High — Thursday's Report Is the Real Test

Private-sector hiring rose by just 98,000 in June, below the 110,000 forecast and down from May's unrevised 122,000, ADP reported Wednesday. "The pace of hiring is telling a story of both supply and demand," said ADP chief economist Nela Richardson. The soft print contrasted with Tuesday's JOLTS data, which showed May job openings climbing to their highest level in two years. Thursday's official June nonfarm payrolls report — moved up a day for the July 4th holiday — is expected to show a 115,000 gain, down from May's 172,000.

Grocery M&A

Kroger to Acquire Giant Eagle for $1.25 Billion Cash Plus Assumed Liabilities

Kroger announced a deal to acquire regional grocer Giant Eagle for $1.25 billion in cash and the assumption of roughly $400 million in liabilities, expanding Kroger's footprint into adjacent Midwest and Mid-Atlantic markets as it competes with Walmart and Amazon amid persistent cost-of-living pressure on grocery shoppers. "We evaluated the opportunity carefully, and the strategic fit is clear," said Kroger CEO Greg Foran.

Federal Reserve & Inflation

Sintra Debut

Warsh Joins Lagarde, Bailey and Macklem in Portugal — No Signal on July, But Plenty of Signal on Style

Wednesday's ECB Forum panel was Kevin Warsh's first public appearance since his June 17 post-FOMC press conference, and his first on the international stage since being confirmed as Fed chair in May. True to his stated aim of minimizing forward guidance, Warsh offered no hint of the Fed's July 28–29 decision, telling moderator Sara Eisen the committee would "get into that room and shut the door" and have "the good debate" without previewing it publicly. He is simultaneously overhauling the Fed's internal operations, having launched five task forces — one specifically examining how the central bank communicates its reasoning. Cleveland Fed President Beth Hammack, speaking separately at the same conference, struck a more explicit tone: "If inflation continues to persist and I don't see any restraint from policy, we may need to raise rates." Markets are now pricing meaningful odds of a Fed hike as early as September, a marked shift from the cutting cycle that dominated late 2025. The ECB, meanwhile, holds its own key rate at 2.25% after a recent quarter-point hike, while the Bank of England and Bank of Canada remain on hold given domestic economic weakness — leaving Warsh's Fed on a notably different, and more hawkish, path than most of its G7 peers. The next FOMC decision lands July 29.

Iran — Doha

Witkoff and Kushner Report "Positive" Talks With Qatar as Technical Implementation Continues

U.S. negotiators Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner met Wednesday with Qatar's prime minister and foreign minister in Doha, part of ongoing indirect talks between Washington and Tehran aimed at converting last month's interim memorandum into a permanent arrangement. A senior U.S. official told Bloomberg the discussions were positive and that progress is being made on technical issues, following a rocky stretch that included a weekend clash near the Strait of Hormuz. Armstrong — Late June

Armstrong's June 29–30 Coverage Tracks Global Yields and a "Corruption" Post as the ECM Framework Persists

Armstrong Economics' June 29 Market Talk documented global sovereign yields drifting mostly higher: the U.S. 10-year rose roughly 1.1 basis points to 4.382%, German Bunds and French OATs ticked up modestly, while UK Gilts eased slightly to 4.728% and Turkish yields remained the standout outlier amid that country's persistent inflation problem. Monday, June 30's post was tagged simply "Corruption," continuing Armstrong's recurring theme that institutional credibility erosion is a leading indicator for his broader Panic Cycle framework. The site's ECM continues to point to 2026 as this cycle's Directional Change year, with an escalating 2027 Panic Cycle and a more severe 2028 Yearly Panic Cycle as the outer structure — the same thesis that has anchored every edition of The Daily Ledger since Vol. I, No. 1.

Real Estate, Mortgage & Tax

Wednesday Rate Check

30-Year Fixed Holds in the Mid-6% Range Across Sources — Variability Index Near Its Lowest of the Year

Mortgage rates were little changed to start July. NerdWallet's APR-based 30-year fixed rate held steady at 6.36%, up 8 basis points from a week ago but 18 basis points below where it stood a year ago. Bankrate and Forbes Advisor's Mortgage Research Center both put the 30-year fixed at 6.47–6.48%, while Optimal Blue data showed conforming 30-year rates at 6.419%. Freddie Mac's benchmark weekly survey last reported 6.49% for the week ending June 25, up slightly from 6.47% the week prior; a fresh Freddie reading is due Thursday at noon ET. Bankrate's Mortgage Rate Variability Index reads just 2 out of 10, indicating unusually little difference between lender offers right now. The MBA forecasts 30-year rates holding near 6.5% through Q3 and Q4, while Fannie Mae projects a slightly softer 6.4% for the balance of the year — either way, housing economists broadly agree rates stay above 6% through year-end.

Tax & Accounting

July 6 Deadline Looms for Retroactive R&E Expense Elections Under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act

The Taxpayer Advocate Service is reminding small businesses that many taxpayers face a July 6, 2026 deadline to retroactively elect new domestic research and experimental (R&E) expense treatment under the One, Big, Beautiful Bill Act, which allows R&E costs paid or incurred after 2024 to be deducted immediately rather than amortized. Businesses with unamortized domestic R&E amounts from tax years 2022–2024 may be able to recover those amounts in a single year or ratably over two, but doing so can require amended returns, Form 3115 accounting-method changes, or Form 6765 research-credit adjustments — the IRS recommends consulting a qualified tax professional before the window closes. Separately, the IRS this week issued Revenue Procedure 2026-25, establishing a gift-tax reporting safe harbor for contributions to the new Trump Accounts created under the same legislation.

Health, Wellness & Fitness

The Real Danger of a Multi-Day Heat Wave Isn't the Daytime High — It's the Overnight Low That Never Comes

With Chicagoland now on day two of an Extreme Heat Warning, local meteorologists are flagging a detail that matters more than the thermometer's peak: overnight lows aren't dropping far enough to let bodies recover. "The heat index value will probably only dip below the 90-degree mark for maybe six, seven hours a night, so recovery is minimal and that's what takes the toll," National Weather Service meteorologist Mike Bardou said this week. Dr. Trevor Lewis, chairman of emergency medicine at Stroger Hospital of Cook County, said seniors are especially at risk because many common medications blunt the body's ability to shed excess heat. Cardiologists note that consecutive days of elevated heat strain the heart even in otherwise healthy adults, since the body diverts blood flow to the skin for cooling, forcing the heart to work harder for longer stretches without the nighttime reprieve it normally gets. The guidance for day two and beyond doesn't change: front-load hydration rather than waiting until thirsty, seek air conditioning for even brief stretches if you lack it at home, and check on older neighbors, since brief AC exposure alone has been shown to meaningfully lower core body temperature during prolonged heat events.

The Daily Triptych

Random Fact

Sintra, the Portuguese hill town hosting this week's ECB Forum, has been a retreat for the powerful since Portuguese royalty first built palaces there in the 19th century to escape Lisbon's summer heat; UNESCO named its entire cultural landscape a World Heritage Site in 1995. The ECB has held its flagship policy forum there annually since 2014, deliberately choosing a setting removed from any single national capital for a gathering meant to look past any one country's politics — fitting, this week, for a debut appearance by a new Fed chair trying to avoid saying anything at all.

Word of the Day
circumspect

(adjective, from Latin circumspicere, "to look around") — wary and unwilling to take risks; carefully considering all circumstances before acting or speaking. It describes both Kevin Warsh's studied refusal to preview Fed policy in Sintra and Iran's negotiators declining to confirm the Doha meeting even as talks visibly proceed — two very different capitals, the same instinct to say less than they know.
