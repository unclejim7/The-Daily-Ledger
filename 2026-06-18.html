Breaking · Geopolitics

Signed, Sealed, Delivered: Trump and Pezeshkian Sign Iran War-Ending MOU at Versailles as Markets Shrug Off Warsh's Hawkish Shock

In a venue switch nobody saw coming, President Trump personally signed the U.S.-Iran memorandum of understanding late Wednesday night during a dinner at the Palace of Versailles marking the 250th anniversary of American independence — not at the Bürgenstock resort in Switzerland, where a ceremonial signing had been expected Friday. Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian signed separately, with Iranian state media releasing an image of him holding the document bearing both leaders' signatures. Pakistani Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif, who mediated alongside Qatar, added his own signature in Islamabad. Leaving Versailles, Trump told reporters simply: "It's signed. Signed in Versailles. Just signed it."

The 14-point agreement calls for an immediate and permanent end to military operations "on all fronts, including in Lebanon," the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, removal of the U.S. naval blockade within 30 days, and the lifting of sanctions tied to Iran's compliance on its enriched-uranium stockpile. A $300 billion reconstruction plan for Iran is also included. Switzerland's foreign ministry confirmed Thursday that the original Bürgenstock gathering will go ahead Friday as planned — not as the signing ceremony, but as the first round of technical talks on implementation, with Pakistan and Qatar still at the table for the 60-day negotiating window that follows.

Markets spent Thursday morning trading the peace, not the Fed. The S&P 500 was up 1.15%, the Nasdaq surged 1.5%, and the Dow added 0.80% in early action, even as the Russell 2000 lagged with a 0.72% loss. Intel led chipmakers higher, up as much as 9.8% premarket, after Trump posted that the company would design and build chips domestically alongside Apple. Crude kept sliding on the reopening news — WTI near $74 a barrel, Brent around $78 — both well off their wartime peak above $114.

That rally is papering over a rougher Wednesday. The Dow hit a fresh intraday record before reversing to close down 507.12 points (-0.98%) at 51,492.55, while the S&P 500 fell 1.21% to 7,420.10 and the Nasdaq dropped 1.34% to 26,021.66, as Kevin Warsh's first FOMC meeting delivered a hawkish jolt: nine of 18 policymakers now project at least one 2026 rate hike, easing-bias language vanished from the statement entirely, and Warsh — a longtime critic of the dot plot — declined to submit one of his own. Bitcoin, for its part, is trading that Fed shock rather than the Iran deal, sliding to $64,198 this morning. Stocks and crypto are telling two different stories about the same week.

The Morning After

Warsh's Debut: A Hold, a Hawkish Dot Plot, and a Statement Half the Usual Length

Wednesday's verdict was exactly what markets expected on the headline — rates held at 3.50%–3.75% — and exactly what they feared underneath it. Nine of the 18 participating Fed officials now pencil in at least one rate hike before the end of 2026, a dramatic reversal from earlier in the year when cuts were still the base case. True to his stated disdain for the dot plot as a communication tool, Warsh declined to submit a projection of his own. The post-meeting statement ran just 130 words, describing the economy as "expanding at a solid pace" and committing the Fed to "deliver price stability" — with any reference to an easing bias stripped out entirely.

Treasury yields surged on the news and have stayed elevated into Thursday, even as equity futures recover some lost ground on the Iran deal. The next scheduled catalyst is several weeks out: May Personal Consumption Expenditures data and an initial Q2 GDP read arrive toward month's end, alongside earnings from Micron and FedEx next week. Until then, Wall Street is left parsing 130 words for clues about how a Warsh-led Fed actually behaves.

Two Markets, One Week

Stocks Are Trading the Peace Deal; Bitcoin Is Trading the Fed — and Losing

The divergence is the story: equities rallied Thursday on the signed Iran MOU while Bitcoin and Ethereum kept sliding, a reminder that crypto has spent the week reacting almost entirely to Wednesday's hawkish dot plot rather than to geopolitics. Bitcoin and Ethereum spot ETFs shed a combined $111 million on Wednesday as rate-cut hopes for 2026 evaporated, though Fidelity's FBTC was a notable exception, pulling in fresh inflows even as the broader fund category retreated. On-chain data continues to tell a steadier story underneath the price action: long-term holders absorbed roughly 125,000 BTC in June, whale wallets holding at least 1,000 BTC have climbed back to 35.82% of available supply, and Strategy now holds 846,842 BTC after adding 1,587 BTC for about $100 million between June 8 and 14. Whether that quiet accumulation outlasts a Fed that just got more hawkish is the question the next CPI print, due in mid-July, may start to answer.

Geopolitics & Current Events

Versailles, Not Bürgenstock

Implementation Talks Still Head to Switzerland Friday — Just Not for the Signing

The surprise Versailles signing leaves Friday's planned Bürgenstock gathering with a new purpose: rather than a ceremonial signing, it now opens the 60-day window of technical negotiations the memorandum requires, with Pakistan and Qatar continuing as mediators alongside the U.S. and Iran. Switzerland's foreign ministry said Thursday the meeting remains on, with roughly 2,000 soldiers securing the resort and a no-fly zone over the mountain in effect through Saturday. Unresolved questions include the precise mechanism for diluting Iran's enriched-uranium stockpile under IAEA supervision and whether removal from Iranian soil will ultimately be required.

Capitol Hill Reaction

Some Republicans Call the Deal a Costly Capitulation

Reaction on Capitol Hill split sharply along familiar lines. Sen. Bill Cassidy called the agreement "the worst foreign policy blunder in decades," arguing Iran's nuclear ambitions went uncurbed while it learned that threatening Hormuz works as leverage. Sen. Thom Tillis tallied the war's cost at roughly $100 billion and two lost F/A-18s. A senior administration official countered that the strikes from Operation Midnight Hammer last year left Iran's accessible enriched material "very, very much buried," undercutting arguments the program remains an immediate threat.

Energy

Brent Settles Near $78 as Hormuz Traffic Resumes

Brent crude has slipped to roughly $78 a barrel and WTI to about $74, both nearly $35 and $24 below their respective wartime peaks, as commercial vessels resume transit through the Strait of Hormuz under the new agreement's terms. France's Macron called the deal "excellent" at the G7 close in Évian, crediting it with restoring "free and toll-free passage" through the strait as of now.

Real Estate & Mortgage Rates

Rate Surveys, Thursday Morning

Mortgage Rates Tick Up as the Hawkish Fed Outweighs the Iran Deal — For Now

NerdWallet's daily average put the 30-year fixed mortgage rate at 6.39% Thursday morning, up seven basis points from Wednesday but still roughly flat with a week ago and 36 basis points below this time last year. Bankrate's survey runs a touch higher at 6.51%, while Freddie Mac's most recent weekly read — through June 11 — had the 30-year averaging 6.52%, up from 6.48% the week before as markets digested the war's lingering effects on oil prices. The split reflects a genuine tug-of-war: the reopening Strait of Hormuz argues for lower energy costs and, eventually, cooler inflation, while Wednesday's dot plot argues for a higher-for-longer Fed. Housing economists continue to expect rates to hold above 6% through year-end regardless of which force wins out this week.

Armstrong Economics

The ECM's June Pivot

Armstrong: The Economic Confidence Model's Downcycle Midpoint Lands This Month

Martin Armstrong's Economic Confidence Model is flagging June 2026 as the midpoint of its current downcycle, with his models projecting a yearlong plunge into mid-2027 from here — timing he says lines up uncomfortably well with a week that delivered both a hawkish Fed surprise and a hastily relocated peace-deal signing. Armstrong continues to frame the broader picture as a series of disconnected "brushfire" wars — Iran, a simmering Taiwan flashpoint, Russia-Ukraine — rather than a single unified conflict, all traceable in his capital-flow models to the same underlying sovereign debt unwind. He's argued Iran's strategy runs on two tracks at once: energy disruption through Hormuz and an attempt to trigger broader financial contagion by targeting banking infrastructure directly, a thesis the timing of this week's events does little to undercut.

On the markets side, Armstrong's long-standing gold and silver calls remain intact even after Tuesday's pullback to a six-month-low close near $4,286 an ounce — he continues to target resistance near $8,500 longer-term, with silver in a $165–$200 range. The nearer-term warning he keeps repeating involves capital controls rather than war itself: Spain's recent cap on withdrawals without government approval is, in his view, exactly the kind of policy that tends to precede broader, digitally enabled restrictions elsewhere as sovereign balance sheets come under strain.

GLP-1 Drugs May Weaken the Link Between Impulsivity and Violence, Study Suggests

A Rutgers study found that GLP-1 medications such as Ozempic and Wegovy appear to weaken the connection between impulsive tendencies and violent behavior, a surprising finding researchers say hints the drugs may influence how people act on impulse more broadly than their metabolic effects alone would suggest.

Daily Probiotic Shows Promise as an Add-On for Depression and Anxiety in Older Adults

A small clinical trial found that older adults with depression who added a daily probiotic to their regular antidepressant treatment experienced slightly greater improvement than those on medication alone, adding to growing interest in the gut-brain connection as a complement to standard psychiatric care.

Early Exposure to a Common Plastic Chemical Linked to Lasting Anxiety in Animal Study

Researchers found that early-life exposure to DEHP, a plasticizer used in everything from medical devices to toys, produced lasting anxiety-related behavioral changes in male rats exposed before and shortly after birth — a reminder that the health effects of everyday plastics are still being mapped.
