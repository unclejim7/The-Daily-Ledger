📈 Markets & Finance
Semiconductor Selloff

Chips Crater — Nasdaq Suffers Worst Day Since April 2025 as Broadcom Disappoints

Friday was ugly. The Nasdaq plunged 4.18%, its worst single-session loss in more than a year, as a savage rotation out of semiconductor names spread from Wall Street to Seoul to Frankfurt. The S&P 500 surrendered 2.64%, closing at 7,383.74 — erasing the record-setting euphoria of Thursday when the Dow had briefly touched all-time highs. The Dow itself fell 695 points, or 1.35%, settling at 50,866.78.

The carnage was concentrated in chipmakers. Broadcom's decision to hold its full-year AI chip revenue targets unchanged — rather than raise them — proved the spark. NVDA lost nearly 6%, Marvell plunged 16%, Micron dropped 13%, AMD and Intel both shed roughly 11%. South Korea's Kospi collapsed 5.54% overnight, with Samsung and SK Hynix each falling 6–8%. ASML in Amsterdam dropped 3.8%. The contagion was global and swift.

Paradoxically, the day also delivered a blockbuster jobs report: nonfarm payrolls rose 172,000 in May — roughly double expectations — with the unemployment rate holding at 4.3%. Strong employment pushed Treasury yields higher, reinforcing the "higher for longer" narrative and giving investors another reason to dump rate-sensitive growth stocks. The 10-year yield moved back toward 4.50%.

🏛️ Federal Reserve
Warsh Era · FOMC June 16–17

Warsh Wants Cuts. The FOMC Wants Data. Friday's Jobs Print Just Changed the Math.

Fed Chair Kevin Warsh was confirmed by the Senate 54–45 on May 13 — the most divisive Fed confirmation in history — and took the helm when Jerome Powell's term expired May 15. Warsh enters his first FOMC meeting as Chair on June 16–17 with a policy agenda that is already running into economic headwinds.

Warsh has publicly positioned himself as leaning toward rate cuts, believing the current Fed Funds rate of 3.50–3.75% is tighter than necessary. But with CPI running at 3.8% annually (its highest since May 2023), a blowout jobs print on Friday, and the 30-year Treasury yield at 5.00%, the committee has made clear it sees no rush. CME FedWatch now prices near-zero probability of a cut at any 2026 meeting, with a non-trivial probability of a hike. The "family fight" Warsh reportedly relished looks set to begin in earnest on June 16.

"The chair has the power to persuade. And they're in a very strong position to be able to persuade. But they still need to persuade."
— Randall Kroszner, former Fed Governor & University of Chicago professor
Rate Outlook

Market Prices No Cut All Year — Hike Risk Rising

CME FedWatch shows less than 3% probability of any 2026 rate cut. Following the hot May jobs data, short-term Treasury yields moved upward and market-implied breakevens rose, adding to the case that Warsh's first FOMC may result in a hold — or even introduce hike language into the statement.

Balance Sheet

QT-for-Cuts Framework Still the Market's Base Case

Warsh's stated preference involves shrinking the Fed balance sheet aggressively (active QT) while eventually lowering short rates. The "bear steepening" of the yield curve this year reflects markets pricing this scenario — long yields elevated, short yields eventually moving lower — though that timeline keeps pushing out.

🌐 Geopolitics
US–Iran · Nuclear Track

Trump Sends Revised Terms to Tehran — Nuclear Deal by June 30 Still a Long Shot

Negotiations between the United States and Iran remain centered on three pillars: extending the April 2026 ceasefire, reopening the Strait of Hormuz, and addressing Iran's nuclear program through a proposed 60-day memorandum of understanding. As of early June, President Trump has sent revised terms demanding firmer written commitments on uranium enrichment and disposal. Iran is reviewing the updated framework through intermediary diplomatic channels.

Trump told reporters Thursday he would be "honored" to meet Iran's Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei — the son and successor of the Ali Khamenei killed in the February US–Israeli strikes — if it led to a deal. Polymarket odds for a formal nuclear agreement by June 30 remain extremely low, reflecting continued uncertainty over enforcement mechanisms, sanctions relief, and phased timelines. Recent military incidents, including sporadic US strikes and Iranian missile activity, have punctuated talks without derailing them.

Background

Pakistan Brokered the April 8 Ceasefire — Extended Indefinitely

After the US–Israeli strikes of late February 2026 and Iran's closure of the Strait of Hormuz, Pakistan mediated a two-week ceasefire that Trump extended indefinitely on April 21. The ceasefire has seen multiple violations from both sides. The Strait remains a central negotiating issue, with US Project Freedom naval operations paused under diplomatic pressure from Pakistan and regional partners.

Oil Markets

Crude Declining After Earlier Spike — Still Inflationary Pressure

WTI crude briefly surged past $98/barrel as the Iran conflict escalated but has been declining in recent sessions. The geopolitical premium remains embedded in energy prices and is a key driver of elevated US CPI data. Any sustainable progress in nuclear talks could trigger a meaningful oil price correction and ease inflation pressure on the Fed.

🏠 Real Estate & Mortgage Rates
Current Rates

30-Year Fixed: 6.59% — Edging Lower But Still Elevated

The average 30-year fixed purchase mortgage rate sits at approximately 6.59% as of June 5 per Zillow data, down slightly from 6.604% on June 4. The Freddie Mac PMMS weekly average published June 1 showed 6.53%. Both measures confirm rates are sticky in the mid-to-upper 6% range — far above the sub-3% lows of 2021.

Market Dynamics

Iran War Premium on Oil Keeping Inflation — and Rates — High

Mortgage rates have trended upward since US war with Iran began in late February, as oil price increases filtered through to CPI. April CPI at 3.8% annually was the highest since May 2023 and caused a rate spike. The MBA's Bob Broeksmit noted purchase demand is still running ahead of last year's pace despite the elevated rate environment.

Outlook

Forecasters See 6.0–6.4% Range Through Mid-2027

Fannie Mae projects 30-year rates averaging 6.3% by Q2 end. The MBA and most institutional forecasters expect rates to remain in the low-to-mid 6% range through at least mid-2027, as stubborn inflation and the Fed's "higher for longer" posture limit how far rates can fall. The lock-in effect continues to suppress resale inventory.

📡 Armstrong Economics

Armstrong Market Talk — Week of June 3–5, 2026

Martin Armstrong's Market Talk posts this week tracked the global bond yield surge and commodity rotations that preceded Friday's equity rout. US 10-year yields pushed to 4.496% (+5 bps) by June 3, with the 30-year crossing 5.00% — a psychologically significant threshold that Armstrong's models flag as consistent with sovereign debt stress building in peripheral European markets. Italy's BTP yield moved to 3.81% (+12 bps), and Turkey spiked to 34.985% (+270 bps) in the same session.

On the commodity front, Armstrong's data showed crude oil gaining 2.65% on June 3 alongside heating oil up 4.11% — energy inflation stubbornly persistent. Notable losers included palladium (−4.38%) and platinum (−3.75%), reflecting industrial demand uncertainty. Armstrong's cyclical framework has long warned that the June 2026 window represents a critical inflection point in the ECM (Economic Confidence Model) downcycle — the current data pattern is consistent with that thesis: sovereign yields rising, equity volatility spiking, and capital rotating defensively.

Armstrong has also noted that the war cycle and the capital flow models intersect at this juncture — with European fiscal stress and Middle East conflict both driving capital into US dollar assets, paradoxically supporting the dollar even as US deficits widen. The Friday jobs print at 172K adds further complexity: a strong labor market makes the case for rate cuts politically and economically harder to construct.

💊 Health & Wellness
Supplements

Biggest Collagen Study Yet: It Actually Works — With Consistency

A major review of nearly 8,000 participants published June 5 found that collagen supplements can improve skin health and ease osteoarthritis symptoms, particularly when taken consistently over longer periods. Researchers also found modest benefits for muscle mass and joint pain. The key qualifier: results required sustained supplementation rather than short-term use.

Cancer Treatment

Scientists Crack "Undruggable" Pancreatic Cancer Target

Researchers announced a breakthrough June 4 targeting the KRAS mutation that drives the majority of pancreatic cancers — a target long considered undruggable. The new drug, daraxonrasib, has shown potential to nearly double survival in early trials. Pancreatic cancer has one of the lowest five-year survival rates of any cancer, making this advance particularly significant.

GLP-1 Drugs

Ozempic Class Drugs Linked to Lower Addiction Risk Too

A massive study of over 600,000 US veterans published June 3 found that GLP-1 drugs like semaglutide may significantly reduce the risk of developing substance use disorders involving alcohol, nicotine, and cannabis — in addition to their known benefits for diabetes and weight loss. The findings add to a growing body of evidence that GLP-1s may reshape the treatment of multiple chronic conditions beyond metabolic disease.
