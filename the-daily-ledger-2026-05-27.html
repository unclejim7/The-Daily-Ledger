⚔ Geopolitics • Top Story

U.S. Strikes Iran Again, Rattling a Peace Deal That Seemed Within Reach

Fresh American military strikes Monday jolted oil markets back above $99 a barrel, just days after diplomatic signals had pushed crude below $96 and sparked hope the three-month war might soon end.

The U.S.-Iran conflict, which began with joint U.S.-Israeli strikes on February 28, has been among the most disruptive geopolitical episodes for global energy in decades. Brent crude surged more than 55% from around $72/bbl at the outset to a peak near $120, though prices have since eased back as both sides floated diplomatic language.

On Monday, the White House confirmed new strikes. Brent touched $100 before pulling back to around $99. By Wednesday morning, crude had retreated further — Brent to $98.16 and WTI to $92.23 — as traders awaited clarity on talks. Secretary of State Marco Rubio said "good signs" of a deal exist, but warned any agreement would be "unfeasible" if Iran pursues permanent control of Strait of Hormuz shipping lanes.

"Energy executives warned that full normalization of Middle East oil supply may not occur until 2027 due to the scale of disruptions caused by the conflict." — MUFG

For American consumers, the war has pushed gas prices up roughly 28% year-over-year and contributed to April's inflation reading of 3.8% — the highest in three years. Mortgage rates climbed in tandem, as bond markets repriced risk. Five Fed speeches are scheduled this week, and Tuesday's Consumer Confidence report will be watched closely for the domestic fallout.

Macroeconomics

Inflation Hits 3-Year High at 3.8%; Fed Holds at 3.50–3.75%

April's consumer price index came in at 3.8% year-over-year — the highest reading in three years — driven largely by a 28% surge in gas prices tied to the Iran conflict. The reading complicates the Federal Reserve's path, as the new Fed Chair (Powell's term expired May 15) navigates twin pressures of persistent inflation and slowing job growth.

Private payroll growth has essentially stalled. Fed Chair Powell noted in March that "effectively, there's zero net job creation in the private sector" on a net-revised basis over six months. The median FOMC dot now projects one additional quarter-point cut in 2026, potentially bringing the funds rate toward 3.25%. The market's next catalyst: Consumer Confidence at 10am ET today, followed by five scheduled Fed speeches through the week.

IRS Filing Season

2026 Filing Season: IRS Claims Success

Of 134 million individual returns filed, 98% were submitted electronically. Fully 80% received refunds, with an average refund of $3,400 — up 11% from 2025. The IRS credits the jump largely to new tip income, overtime, and car loan interest deductions under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA). Paper filers, however, faced significantly longer processing delays as the IRS accelerates its check-free transition.

OBBBA Guidance

IRS Issues Final Tip Deduction Regs; Overtime & Car Loan Rules Create Return Problems

Final regulations on the tip income deduction were issued April 10, effective June 12, 2026. The IRS continued rolling out OBBBA guidance throughout the filing season, but the late timing created friction. Practitioners are flagging significant reconciliation problems with tip, overtime, and car loan interest deductions — primarily because the amounts claimed by taxpayers cannot be reconciled with employer-reported information or lender-issued 1099s.

In calculating the overtime deduction specifically, some taxpayers appear to have mischaracterized regular hours as overtime, triggering downstream issues. Expect IRS correspondence and potential audit activity in the back half of 2026 on these deductions.

⚠ Court Alert — Closely Held C Corps

Dillon Trust Co. v. United States — A Cautionary Tale for Stock Sale Transactions

The Federal Circuit's May 14 decision in Dillon Trust Co. LLC v. United States serves as a stark warning for tax practitioners handling the sale of closely held C corporations with highly appreciated assets. The court held that sellers cannot turn a blind eye to economically irrational bids without risking massive transferee liability, penalties, and interest — particularly when the buyer is an intermediary exploiting an abusive tax shelter structure. Willful blindness is no defense. Review all pending stock sale transactions for red flags.
