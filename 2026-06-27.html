Technology · Markets · Weekend Edition

OpenAI Blinks: IPO Pushed to 2027 as SpaceX’s 32% Post-Debut Collapse Kills Appetite for Trillion-Dollar AI Debuts — The Week the AI Bubble Started Leaking

The New York Times report that landed Thursday evening was the pin that deflated what remained of the AI market’s peak euphoria: OpenAI is leaning toward delaying its initial public offering until 2027. The proximate cause was SpaceX. Priced at $135 per share on June 12, SPCX rocketed to an intraday peak above $225 before a swift, steep retracement brought it back to approximately $152.86 by June 26 — nearly 32% off its high in less than two weeks. That trajectory told OpenAI’s board everything it needed to know about what a $1 trillion debut would face in a market that has spent June systematically questioning every AI valuation assumption it once held as settled.

The internal dynamics are more revealing than the headline. CEO Sam Altman has reportedly refused to authorize any IPO at a valuation below $1 trillion — a figure well above OpenAI’s most recent private valuation of $730–850 billion. CFO Sarah Friar has argued for waiting until 2027, citing ongoing cash burn, compute infrastructure commitments running into the tens of billions annually, and the quarterly public reporting burden against a financial profile that remains deeply unprofitable. OpenAI filed confidentially with the SEC on June 8 but emphasized timing was flexible. Advisors presented two options: delay and wait for the trillion-dollar moment, or lower the target and move now. Altman chose 2027. The advisory team’s warning that retail investors are “highly unlikely to show much enthusiasm” for OpenAI stock at current valuations reflects a broader market shift that this week made impossible to ignore.

The fallout hit SoftBank first and hardest. Masayoshi Son’s firm holds approximately $65 billion in OpenAI equity — roughly a 13% stake — after co-leading the company’s $122 billion funding round in March 2026. SoftBank shares plunged 12.5% on the OpenAI delay news, wiping out nearly 5.6 trillion yen in market value. The ripple hit Asian tech broadly: the KOSPI fell 5.8% Friday as Samsung and SK Hynix declined again.

Friday’s US session confirmed the damage. The Nasdaq posted its fifth consecutive losing session, dropping 0.24% to close at 25,297.62 — its longest losing streak since February. The S&P 500 ticked down 0.05% to 7,354.02. For the week, the Nasdaq lost 4.6% — its worst week since April — while the S&P declined nearly 2%. The Dow, buoyed by Microsoft (+5.7%), Salesforce (+5.4%), and IBM (+5.1%), managed a 0.6% weekly gain.

Tax & Accounting

Q2 Closes Sunday June 29

The Tax-Loss Harvesting Window Is Open Right Now — Bitcoin Down 45% From ATH, AI Stocks Off Their Highs, Q2 Ends Sunday

Sunday, June 29 closes the second quarter and with it a practical decision window many investors overlook. Bitcoin is down nearly 45% from its October 2025 all-time high and approximately 19% over the past month. For taxable accounts, these unrealized losses represent tax-loss harvesting opportunities. The mechanics: selling a position that is down and realizing the loss allows you to offset capital gains elsewhere in the portfolio, or deduct up to $3,000 of net capital losses against ordinary income annually. The IRS wash-sale rule prohibits repurchasing the substantially identical security within 30 days — but you can immediately replace a Bitcoin ETF with a different Bitcoin ETF, or swap Micron for Applied Materials. Note: this applies only to taxable brokerage accounts. Q2 ends Sunday. The window closes Monday.

Word of the Day
capitulation

(noun, from French: capituler, “to make terms”) — in financial markets, the point at which a critical mass of investors abandon hope of price recovery and sell at a loss, producing a sharp final decline that often marks a cycle bottom. The current Bitcoin Fear & Greed Index at 13 is historically associated with capitulation-adjacent conditions.
