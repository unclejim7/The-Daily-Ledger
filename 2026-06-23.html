Markets · Technology · Tuesday Edition

The AI Gravity Check: SpaceX Slides, Korea Craters, and the Nasdaq Faces Its Worst Day Since the IPO Selloff Began

Global markets delivered a blunt reality check Tuesday morning, with South Korea’s KOSPI plummeting 10% — its steepest single-session drop in more than three months — as overseas investors dumped chip heavyweights Samsung and SK Hynix by more than 12% each, triggering an automatic 20-minute trading halt. The sell-off, which spread to European tech stocks (Stoxx 600 Technology −3%, STMicro and ASMI down 7%+) and fueled Nasdaq 100 futures losses of 1.66% before the U.S. open, is a continuation of a rotation that began when Alphabet fell 5% Monday on AI infrastructure cost concerns and SpaceX (SPCX) shed another 16% in its worst single-session decline since its record-setting June 12 IPO. SpaceX has now erased more than $900 billion in market value from its all-time high of $225.64, reached just one week ago.

The proximate catalysts are multiple and interlocking. SpaceX’s announcement of a $20 billion bond sale — its first debt offering since going public — rattled a market already nervous about AI capital expenditure at scale. Oracle simultaneously disclosed plans to cut roughly 13% of its global workforce (approximately 18,000 jobs), explicitly citing AI automation as the driver, at a restructuring cost of $1.8 billion. Amazon, Meta, and Palantir fell 4% Monday alongside Alphabet’s 5% decline, as investors began questioning whether AI hyperscaler capex is creating revenue fast enough to justify valuations. Dan Ives of Wedbush called the selloff a “gut check moment” and a buying opportunity, noting that AI revolution is still in “the third inning.” Structurally, the Nasdaq-100 rebalancing for SpaceX is expected around July 6, which will force QQQ and tracker funds to mechanically buy SPCX by trimming existing holdings — a second wave of index pressure that few are discussing.

Monday’s U.S. session closed mixed: the Dow gained 0.29% to 51,712.71 while the S&P 500 dipped 0.37% to 7,472.79 and the Nasdaq fell 1.32% to 26,166.60. Tuesday premarket has Dow futures down 0.32%, S&P futures off 0.91%, and Nasdaq futures down a sharp 1.66%. Bitcoin slipped to $62,325 overnight, down from $64,080 on Monday, as risk aversion extended into crypto. The macro calendar this week remains anchored by Friday’s May PCE print — the Fed’s preferred inflation gauge — and Micron’s fiscal Q3 earnings tomorrow after the close, where the Street expects a near-record $19.72 EPS on $34.52 billion in revenue. Micron is the AI memory proxy that could either stabilize or accelerate the tech rout depending on what its HBM guidance says.

The geopolitical backdrop remains constructive: the Bürgenstock 60-day Iran roadmap is intact, WTI crude fell further to $73.10, and the 10-year Treasury yield edged up to 4.511% per Monday’s Armstrong Market Talk data. The UK political transition continues in its interregnum as Andy Burnham consolidates frontrunner status for Labour leadership. But the market story of Tuesday is unmistakably tech-driven, with South Korean contagion proving that the post-SpaceX-IPO AI enthusiasm has encountered its most serious credibility test yet.

KOSPI Contagion

South Korea’s KOSPI Drops 10% — Samsung & SK Hynix Each Tumble 12%+ on AI Valuation Fears

South Korean equities collapsed Tuesday as overseas investors fled chip heavyweights at historic velocity. A 20-minute circuit breaker was triggered at the exchange. The KOSPI drop of roughly 10% is its steepest in more than three months, and the declines in Samsung and SK Hynix — which had been at record highs on the AI memory trade — erased hundreds of billions in market value in a single session. European chipmakers followed: STMicroelectronics and ASMI each fell 7%+ on the Stoxx 600.

SpaceX Spiral

SPCX Down 24% From ATH — $20B Bond Sale, Alphabet Rout, and AI Capex Doubt Drive the Slide

SpaceX fell 16.43% Monday and is extending losses in Tuesday premarket, having shed more than $900 billion from its June 16 all-time high of $225.64. The company announced a $20 billion senior notes offering to fund its AI ambitions, which rattled a market that had already grown nervous about Alphabet’s $80 billion AI infrastructure raise and Oracle’s mass layoffs. The stock’s Nasdaq-100 inclusion around July 6 will force QQQ to mechanically buy SPCX — creating a second wave of selling pressure in existing holdings as funds rebalance.

Micron Tomorrow

Fiscal Q3 Earnings Wednesday After Close — $34.52B Revenue, $19.72 EPS Expected; HBM Guidance Is Everything

All eyes in the semiconductor sector turn to Micron’s fiscal Q3 report after the close Wednesday. The Street consensus projects $34.52 billion in revenue and $19.72 EPS — a number that would have seemed impossible 18 months ago. Micron guided for roughly 81% gross margins in Q3. The critical variables: HBM4 capacity commitments, Nvidia’s Vera Rubin demand signals, and whether the company raises its FY2027 outlook. A strong print could halt the AI memory selloff; a miss or cautious guidance could deepen it. Micron Q2 revenue was $23.86 billion; the Q3 jump to $34.5B would be the most dramatic single-quarter ramp in the company’s history.

Oracle Layoffs

Oracle Cuts 13% of Its Workforce — 18,000 Jobs Eliminated, $1.8B in Restructuring Costs

Oracle disclosed plans to reduce its global headcount by nearly 13%, from 162,000 employees a year ago to 141,000. The company cited AI adoption and deployment as the driver, noting that AI technologies have “resulted, and may continue to result, in reductions to our workforce.” The filing disclosed $1.8 billion in restructuring costs — a sharp increase from $374 million the prior year. The disclosure arrived alongside Oracle’s earnings and adds fuel to the AI-disrupts-labor narrative playing out across enterprise technology.

Health, Wellness & Fitness

The “AI Is Taking Our Jobs” Headline and the Psychology of Economic Uncertainty

Oracle’s disclosure that it eliminated 18,000 jobs while explicitly crediting AI automation landed Tuesday in a week already loaded with tech-sector anxiety. Research in occupational health psychology consistently documents that news of mass layoffs — even in industries or companies disconnected from one’s own work — activates anticipatory threat responses and elevates ambient cortisol in population-level studies. The practical response is not catastrophizing but calibration: understanding which skills are complementary to AI tools (judgment, client relationships, domain expertise, novel problem-framing) versus which are most exposed to automation (repetitive high-volume processing, commodity analysis). For knowledge workers, the current moment rewards investing in the former and systematically building fluency in the latter.

Random Fact

South Korea’s KOSPI index contains a built-in circuit breaker mechanism that automatically halts trading for 20 minutes whenever the index falls 8% or more in a single session — a rule introduced after the 1997 Asian financial crisis. Tuesday’s 10% intraday drop triggered it for the first time in nearly four months. SK Hynix, whose Monday valuation briefly surpassed Samsung’s to make it South Korea’s most valuable listed company, gave back much of that gain in a single session — a swing that underscores just how narrowly the AI memory trade’s premium has been priced.

Word of the Day
contagion

(noun, Latin origin: contagio, “contact, infection”) — the spread of a financial shock, behavioral panic, or disease from one market, institution, or population to another through direct or indirect connection. In economics, financial contagion describes how a crisis in one country or sector can propagate globally through trade linkages, shared creditors, or investor sentiment. Tuesday’s simultaneous drops in Seoul, Frankfurt, and Nasdaq futures are a textbook demonstration: a valuation correction in U.S. AI names became a global chip sector rout within 24 hours.
