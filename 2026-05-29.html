Markets
S&P Posts 9th Straight Weekly Win at Record Highs — May Historic for Bulls
The S&P 500 closed Friday at a record high, extending its winning streak to nine consecutive weeks — the longest since late 2023. Dell Technologies' extraordinary 33% single-session gain Thursday anchored a week that saw the Nasdaq add 8.1% for May, its best monthly performance since 2003. The Dow Jones crossed 51,000. With the month closing at all-time highs, the bull case is simple: AI is real, corporate earnings are beating, and global capital has nowhere better to go. PCE inflation at 3.8% is a complicating factor, but the market has decided to look through it for now. The bears have been wrong nine weeks running.

When the 30-year crosses 5%, it historically creates headwinds for growth stocks. So far equities are ignoring the signal — but that has limits.

Macro & Federal Reserve

Federal Reserve

Warsh: "Credibility Cannot Be Borrowed — Only Earned"

In his confirmation testimony, Kevin Warsh delivered a clear message: the Fed's credibility on inflation has been damaged and must be rebuilt through consistent, rule-based policy. He signaled deep skepticism of discretionary easing and expressed concern about fiscal dominance — the idea that rising government debt could eventually force the Fed's hand on rates.

Consumer Economy

Dimon Warns of "Credit Recession" — Lower-Income Consumers Squeezed

JPMorgan CEO Jamie Dimon warned this week that a "credit recession" is underway among lower-income American households. Delinquency rates on auto loans and credit cards are at 10-year highs for borrowers under $50K income. The bifurcated consumer — wealthy households fine, lower-income households strained — is a recurring theme in 2026 data.

Current Events

Iran
Iran MOU Text Agreed — Trump Adding Tougher Verification Demands
Negotiators in Muscat have agreed on the core text of a memorandum of understanding to end the U.S.–Iran military conflict. However, President Trump is personally editing the document to add stricter verification requirements for Iran's nuclear program and explicit restrictions on its ballistic missile development — provisions that Iranian negotiators have signaled could be dealbreakers. The standoff has been simmering since late March and has cost more than 400 combined military and civilian lives while pushing oil markets into a war-premium regime. A deal would be a major positive catalyst for equity markets and would push WTI crude sharply lower. Analysts are calling this a "days, not weeks" window — though prior deadlines have slipped before.

Real Estate

National

Spring Market Stalls — Listings Outpace Buyers for First Time

Active listings nationally are outpacing signed contracts for the first time since 2019 in the spring buying season. High mortgage rates — the 30-year is at 6.59% on Zillow — are keeping would-be buyers on the sidelines. Price reductions are appearing in markets that were bulletproof just 18 months ago.

Chicago

Fannie Mae Sees Rates at 6.1–6.3% All Year — No Relief Coming

Fannie Mae's May forecast holds the 30-year fixed rate at 6.1–6.3% for the remainder of 2026 — meaning the affordability squeeze is not resolving this year. For Chicago-area buyers, the math remains difficult: a $400,000 home at 6.5% carries a principal and interest payment roughly $800/month higher than it would have at 3%.

Tax & Accounting

OBBBA Passes House Narrowly — Senate Battle Begins

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act cleared the House on a narrow party-line vote and now enters what will be a difficult Senate journey. The bill's key tax provisions — extending 2017 TCJA cuts, raising the SALT deduction cap to approximately $40,000, restoring PMI deductibility, and extending the 20% passthrough deduction — are all popular but expensive. Senate deficit hawks led by several Republican senators are signaling they want modifications. Illinois taxpayers and CPAs should be modeling both the current-law baseline and the OBBBA scenario for any significant financial decisions this year. The effective dates, if the bill passes, are likely retroactive to January 1, 2026.

Armstrong Economics

Armstrong: May Pivot Confirmed — Summer Test Ahead

Martin Armstrong's Socrates system flagged May 2026 as a key cyclical inflection point for global capital, and the month is closing with confirmation: equities at all-time highs, capital flowing into dollar assets, and the Iran conflict nearing resolution. Armstrong's view is that the current bull market is being sustained by capital flight from European sovereign instability and geopolitical risk — not by Fed easing. His model continues to flag September–October 2026 as the next significant test for U.S. equity markets. He sees any summer pullback as a buying opportunity within the larger bull cycle he projects running to late 2027. Armstrong remains skeptical of the bond market and sees the long end of the curve as the primary systemic vulnerability globally.

Health & Wellness

Longevity

VO2 Max Is the Single Best Predictor of Lifespan

A major meta-analysis confirmed what exercise physiologists have long suspected: cardiorespiratory fitness, measured by VO2 max, predicts all-cause mortality more reliably than any blood marker, including cholesterol, blood pressure, or fasting glucose. Each 1-unit increase in VO2 max is associated with a 9% reduction in mortality risk.

Cognitive Health

Ultra-Processed Foods Accelerate Brain Aging in 40s

Harvard researchers published findings showing that adults in their 40s who regularly consumed ultra-processed foods showed measurably greater white matter decline on MRI than matched controls eating whole foods — even after controlling for total caloric intake. The effect was dose-dependent and appeared to be driven by food additives rather than macronutrient content.

Random Fact

Chicago's "L" Is the Second Oldest Elevated Rail in the US

Chicago's elevated rail system — universally known as "The L" — opened its first section in 1892, making it the second oldest elevated rapid transit system in the United States after New York's. The iconic Loop section of elevated track, circling the downtown business district, opened in 1897 and gave the neighborhood its name.

Word of the Day

Fiscal Dominance

A situation where a government's large debt obligations constrain the central bank's ability to raise interest rates, because higher rates would make the debt unserviceable. Kevin Warsh specifically warned against this in his confirmation testimony — it's one of the central risks in the current macro environment.
