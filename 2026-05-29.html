Breaking · U.S.–Iran Diplomacy

Deal on Hormuz "Largely Negotiated" — Markets Surge on Peace Hopes

Stocks opened Friday at fresh all-time highs and oil prices slid as traders digested reports that the United States and Iran have reached a tentative framework agreement that could reopen the Strait of Hormuz and set a 30-to-60-day diplomatic clock ticking toward broader nuclear talks. President Trump declared on Truth Social that a deal is "largely negotiated, subject to finalization," and early Friday reports indicated a memorandum of understanding was being drafted as a Phase One agreement.

Treasury Secretary Bessent confirmed the outline but cautioned that any final deal would still require Iran to surrender its highly enriched uranium stockpile, commit to no nuclear program, and allow free navigation through the strait — a $4 trillion chokepoint for global energy that has been a flashpoint since late February. Vice President Vance said Friday morning that "a couple of language points" remain unresolved, tempering enthusiasm but leaving markets largely optimistic. Asian equities gained nearly 1%, leaving MSCI's regional index just short of a record. WTI crude fell roughly 1% to approximately $87 a barrel; Brent dropped to around $91.

🏦 Macroeconomics & Federal Reserve

Inflation · PCE Report

Inflation Hits 3-Year High: PCE Jumps to 3.8% in April

Thursday's Commerce Department data delivered a sobering reminder of the inflationary pressure still gripping the U.S. economy. The Personal Consumption Expenditures price index rose 0.4% in April and climbed to 3.8% on an annual basis — the highest reading in nearly three years — driven largely by the oil price shock stemming from the Iran conflict. Core PCE (ex-food and energy) rose a slightly cooler-than-expected 0.2% for the month but still landed at 3.3% year-over-year.

The underlying consumer picture was equally sobering: personal income was flat for the month, real disposable income fell 0.5%, and the personal savings rate dropped to 2.6% — its lowest since 2022. Americans have been drawing down savings to absorb elevated gas prices, a situation economists warn is not indefinitely sustainable. Real consumer spending grew just 0.1% after inflation adjustments, even as nominal spending rose 0.5%.

First-quarter GDP was also revised lower, to an annualized rate of 1.6%, below the initial 2.0% estimate, due to downward revisions to consumer spending and investment.

Federal Reserve · Leadership Transition

Kevin Warsh Takes the Helm at the Fed

Kevin Warsh was sworn in on May 22 as the 17th chair of the Federal Reserve, succeeding Jerome Powell in a contentious, near-party-line Senate vote of 54–45 — the most divisive in Fed history. Trump hosted the ceremony in the White House East Room, with Justice Clarence Thomas administering the oath. Powell remains on the Fed Board as a governor through early 2028.

Warsh, 56 and former governor during the 2008 financial crisis, takes control with headline PCE at 3.8%, tariff-driven price pressures persistent, and Trump publicly demanding rate cuts. His first meeting as chair is set for June 16–17. The Fed funds rate remains at 3.50–3.75%. Markets are watching closely for any signal on timing of cuts — and how vigorously Warsh guards the central bank's political independence.

📋 Tax & Accounting

Tax Policy · Farm Income

Farmer Bridge Assistance Program Payments End, Dragging Income Data

April's personal income report was flattered by the absence of farm income payments from the Farmer Bridge Assistance Program — a one-time payment initiative tied to the new farm bill provisions under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act. The BEA noted these payments had temporarily boosted income figures in prior months; their cessation contributed to April's flat personal income reading and the 0.5% decline in real disposable income.

Private-sector wages and salaries rose 0.25% in April and are up 3.86% year-over-year — roughly in line with headline PCE inflation of 3.8%, meaning real wages are essentially flat. For tax planning purposes, the combination of high inflation and nominal wage growth tracking at near-PCE levels creates a challenging real-return environment, particularly for fixed-income-heavy retirement portfolios.

🏠 Real Estate

Housing Market · May 2026

Pending Sales Rise, Prices Hit $417,700 Record — But Is the Rally Sustainable?

The May housing picture is decidedly mixed. Pending home sales rose a surprising 1.4% in April, defying expectations amid elevated mortgage rates. Nationwide inventory climbed 4.2% year-over-year to approximately 1.23 million active listings, and the median existing home price reached a record $417,700. Homes in peak seasonal form are averaging around 30 days on market.

Yet the optimism is tempered by context. The 30-year fixed mortgage rate remains elevated — forecasts from Fannie Mae point to a potential dip below 6% (to roughly 5.7%) by year-end, but near-term volatility tied to Iran tensions and inflation keeps rates sticky. About 40% of buyers and sellers surveyed by BiggerPockets express concern about a housing market crash — though economists broadly characterize the current environment as a "rebalancing, not a collapse." Regional divergence is stark: Sun Belt markets with high inventory face genuine softness, while inventory-starved Midwestern cities like Chicago remain relatively tight.

Supercharged Vitamin K Compound May Help the Brain Regenerate Neurons

Japanese scientists have engineered powerful new vitamin K-based compounds that show early promise in helping the brain regenerate lost neurons. The research, published May 27, could eventually have implications for neurodegenerative conditions like Parkinson's and Alzheimer's — though human trials remain years away.

Mental Health · Immunology

New Anti-Inflammatory Drug Approach Shows Early Promise for Depression

A small clinical trial published May 28 found that an anti-inflammatory drug — typically used for autoimmune conditions — produced measurable improvements in patients with treatment-resistant depression. The finding supports a growing theory that some forms of depression may be rooted in immune system dysregulation rather than purely in neurotransmitter chemistry.

Nutrition · Gut-Brain Axis

Scientists Discover Hidden Gut-Brain Circuit That Drives Protein Cravings

When the body runs short on protein, the gut sends powerful signals to the brain that reshape cravings — pushing toward amino-acid-rich foods and away from sugar, according to new research published this week. The newly described gut-brain network could inform future strategies for metabolic health, appetite regulation, and protein-focused dietary interventions.

Long COVID · Brain Imaging

Brain Scans Find No Widespread Inflammation in Long COVID Patients

A new imaging study published May 28 found no evidence of broad neuroinflammation in patients experiencing prolonged post-COVID symptoms — contradicting one popular hypothesis about long COVID's mechanism. Researchers say the most severe symptoms may instead involve disrupted neural circuitry or autonomic nervous system dysregulation rather than active inflammation.
