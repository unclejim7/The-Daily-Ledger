Weekend Wrap

Week Ends on Sour Note: Nasdaq Falls 4.7% as Chip Selloff Accelerates on Hot Jobs Data

The week of June 2–6 began with the Dow hitting an all-time high before reversing sharply on Friday following an unexpectedly strong May employment report. The Nasdaq fell 4.2% on Friday alone — its worst single-session loss since April 2025 — while the S&P 500 shed 2.6% and the Dow dropped 1.4%. For the week, the Nasdaq lost approximately 4.7% and the S&P fell over 2%. The culprit: 172,000 new jobs in May, roughly double consensus estimates. Strong labor data pushed Treasury yields above 4.5% on the 10-year and past 5% on the 30-year, reigniting fears that the Federal Reserve will keep rates higher for longer — or possibly hike.

Iran-Israel Ceasefire Increasingly Fragile on Day 99

The ceasefire agreed April 7–8 between the U.S., Israel, and Iran continued to fray through the weekend. Hezbollah rejected a U.S.-brokered deal with Lebanon, and Israeli strikes on Beirut's southern suburbs on Sunday set the stage for the Monday escalation. The 2026 Iran war — initiated February 28 by U.S. and Israeli airstrikes — remains the defining geopolitical event of the year, disrupting global trade, oil markets, and flight routes across the Middle East.

Editor's Dispatch

Friday's selloff was a reminder that "good news" for the economy is increasingly "bad news" for markets when the Fed is stuck in neutral and inflation refuses to cooperate. The jobs data was strong — unusually so. That's not nothing. But the bond market's reaction told the real story: yields spiked, stocks skidded, and chip stocks took a particular beating. Heading into Monday with Mideast tensions rising and May CPI on deck for Tuesday June 10, it's a setup that rewards staying awake.

Word of the Day
Stagflation

(noun) An economic condition marked by slow growth, high unemployment, and rising prices simultaneously — the nightmare scenario that the Fed is currently trying to avoid as strong jobs data coexists with elevated inflation driven by geopolitical energy shocks.
