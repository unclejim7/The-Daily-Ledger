Market Wreckage — Week's Aftermath

Worst Week for Tech Since April 2025: Chips Crater, Jobs Surge, and the 30-Year Yield Hits 5%

Friday's close left Wall Street in a state of shock. The Nasdaq Composite fell 4.18% — its worst single session since the tariff turbulence of April 2025 — as a semiconductor sell-off that started with Broadcom's underwhelming AI chip forecast amplified into a global rout. The S&P 500 surrendered 2.64%, settling at 7,383.74. The Dow shed 695 points, or 1.35%, at 50,866.78 — a grim bookend to Thursday's all-time intraday record. The VIX fear index rocketed 39.68% to 21.51, signaling a return of volatility after months of relative calm.

The catalyst was Broadcom's earnings: not a disaster, but a refusal to raise its AI chip revenue forecast — and in a market priced for perpetual AI upside, that was enough. Marvell Technology plunged 16%, Micron fell 13%, AMD and Intel each shed roughly 11%, Nvidia lost 6.2%. The contagion spread globally: South Korea's Kospi dropped 5.54%, ASML in Amsterdam fell 3.8%, and Germany's Infineon shed 6%. The semiconductor selloff was simultaneous across time zones.

Overlaid on this was a bombshell jobs report. May nonfarm payrolls came in at 172,000 — roughly double what forecasters expected — with unemployment holding at 4.3%. Strong labor data immediately pushed Treasury yields higher. The 10-year note surged 6.6 basis points to 4.547%, its highest since May 21. The 30-year crossed 5.01%. Hike odds for year-end, per CME FedWatch, climbed to 43%. The "higher for longer" era is not going anywhere.

Jobs Report — May 2026

172K New Jobs, Unemployment at 4.3% — Double the Consensus Forecast

The Bureau of Labor Statistics reported 172,000 nonfarm payrolls added in May — roughly twice what Wall Street expected. Unemployment held at 4.3%. Job gains were concentrated in health care, construction, and transportation. The blowout print extinguished any residual hope of Fed rate cuts and pushed short-term yields to their highest level since February 2025.

🏛️ Federal Reserve
Warsh's First FOMC · June 16–17

Hot Jobs Print Torches the Cuts Case — Warsh Heads Into First FOMC with Both Hands Tied

New Fed Chair Kevin Warsh — who took the helm when Jerome Powell's term expired May 15 — enters his first FOMC meeting on June 16–17 with the policy agenda he campaigned on in serious trouble. Warsh has publicly signaled a preference for rate cuts, framing the current Fed Funds rate of 3.50–3.75% as unnecessarily restrictive. But the data won't cooperate.

Friday's jobs report pushed CME FedWatch hike odds for December 2026 to 43% — and cut odds to near zero. The April FOMC minutes, published via the Fed's official website, confirmed the committee voted to hold rates at 3.50–3.75% and set the next meeting for June 16–17. The FOMC noted "elevated inflation" and what it described as weakening job trends — though Friday's blowout print significantly complicates that narrative. J.P. Morgan's research team now sees the Fed holding all year, with the next move more likely to be a hike in Q3 2027 than any 2026 cut.

"There is no argument for Fed rate cuts with the labor market this strong, and Fed officials must concentrate on the inflation risks because the economy is heating up."
— Christopher Rupkey, Chief Economist, Fwdbonds
Rate Levels

Fed Funds: 3.50–3.75% · Reserve Rate: 3.65% · Primary Credit: 3.75%

The April 29 FOMC vote confirmed these rates unanimously. Yield Curve

The bear-steepening yield curve mirrors Armstrong Economics' forecast: the long end rising faster than the short end as fiscal concerns and geopolitical inflation premiums compound. The curve now reflects genuine stagflation risk embedded in bond market pricing.

Supplements — New Research

Vitamins B12 & D: Helpful Only If You're Deficient — Study Warns Against Blanket Use

A June 6 review confirms that while vitamins B12 and D can play a critical role for older adults, they are most beneficial when a specific deficiency exists — not as preventive supplements for everyone. Protein supplementation showed more universal benefit. The research underscores a growing clinical consensus: targeted supplementation based on actual deficiency testing outperforms the "just take everything" approach that drives billions in annual supplement sales.

Collagen Science

Largest Collagen Review Yet — 8,000 Participants Confirm Real Benefits

A landmark meta-analysis published June 5 covering nearly 8,000 participants found that collagen supplements meaningfully improve skin health and reduce osteoarthritis symptoms — when taken consistently over extended periods. Modest but measurable benefits for muscle and joint function were also confirmed. Researchers emphasized that consistency is the key variable: short-term use showed significantly weaker results than sustained supplementation.

Longevity

Six Science-Backed Pillars for Long-Term Health — No Shortcuts Required
