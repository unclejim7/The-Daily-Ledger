Breaking · Federal Reserve

Decision Day: Warsh Delivers First Rate Verdict at 2 PM as Bond Market Bets Shift From Cuts to Hikes

Kevin Warsh's first interest-rate decision as Federal Reserve chair lands today at 2:00 PM Eastern, with his debut press conference following at 2:30 PM. The outcome itself is barely in question — CME FedWatch puts the odds of a hold at 3.50%–3.75% at roughly 97% — but the meeting carries unusual weight because it is also a quarterly projections meeting, meaning Warsh's first updated Summary of Economic Projections and dot plot land alongside the statement. Analysts widely expect the committee to drop the single rate cut March's dot plot had penciled in for 2026, effectively signaling a hold-through-year-end stance, with Bank of America flagging at least three members likely to project an outright 2026 hike.

The market backdrop has shifted markedly in Warsh's favor for a hawkish framing. Prediction markets now assign roughly a 50.5% probability to at least one Fed rate hike before year-end, a near-total reversal from January's expectations of multiple cuts, driven by May's hot 4.2% CPI print and producer prices running near 6%. A Bank of America survey found 55% of respondents expect Warsh to strike a hawkish tone in his first press conference, even though the bank's own house view is that Warsh will lean dovish relative to the rest of the committee. That tension — a chair who has spoken of running "messier meetings" presiding over a panel split between hawks and a White House that has said it "loved" the inflation print — is the real story Wednesday afternoon will tell.

Tuesday's market action previewed the crosscurrents. The Dow Jones Industrial Average closed at a record 51,999.67, up 0.64%, lifted by JPMorgan, Visa and 3M, while the S&P 500 slipped 0.57% to 7,511.35 and the Nasdaq fell 1.15% to 26,376.34 as traders locked in profits in chipmakers — Nvidia, Broadcom, Micron, AMD and Intel all fell — following their recent run. SpaceX bucked the trend, rising another 4.8% after confirming it will spend $60 billion to acquire AI coding startup Cursor, a deal that would absorb a large share of the company's recent IPO proceeds.

One disinflationary wildcard working in the dovish camp's favor: crude oil has kept sliding toward $75 a barrel as shipping resumes through the Strait of Hormuz, removing one of the clearest arguments for tightening further. Whether that's enough to keep Warsh's committee from leaning hawkish this afternoon is the question Wall Street will be watching the dot plot — and Warsh's own words — to answer.

Federal Reserve

What to Watch at 2 PM

Four Questions the Dot Plot and Statement Need to Answer

Beyond the all-but-certain hold, four things separate a routine meeting from a market-moving one: does the 2026 median dot drop the last projected rate cut, confirming a hold-through-year-end path; does any individual dot shift higher, formally putting a 2026 hike on the table; does Warsh choose to omit his own dot from the projections entirely, a break from tradition some expect given his "less is more" approach to forward guidance; and does the post-meeting statement finally drop its long-standing easing-bias language. JPMorgan Wealth Management's Phil Camporeale has said he expects an explicit shift from an easing bias to a neutral stance — itself a meaningful signal about how differently Warsh intends to run the committee compared to predecessor Jerome Powell, who remains on the board through the transition.

The last time the Fed actually moved rates was December's cut, which set the current range; January, March and April all produced holds, with April's 8-4 vote the most divided FOMC decision since 1992. That divide is exactly what Warsh inherits today, and exactly why a new chair's first meeting tends to draw outsized scrutiny — markets have no history yet with his communication style, and every word choice this afternoon will be read for what it signals about meetings to come.

Iran Deal · Venue Confirmed

Switzerland Locks In Bürgenstock Resort for Friday's Signing — Trump's Attendance Still Uncertain

Switzerland's Foreign Ministry has confirmed the U.S.-Iran memorandum of understanding will be signed Friday at the Bürgenstock resort, a heavily secured property perched above Lake Lucerne with water on three sides, chosen jointly by Pakistani and Qatari mediators along with the U.S. and Iran. President Trump told reporters at the G7 summit he may not attend personally — "I may be involved; I may not," he said — while confirming Vice President JD Vance's presence is locked in. The signing kicks off a 60-day window for follow-up technical talks on the harder unresolved questions: Iran's nuclear program, sanctions relief, and the precise terms governing freedom of navigation through the Strait of Hormuz, which the International Energy Agency has stressed must reopen fully to support the global economic recovery from the conflict's energy-price shock.

Real Estate & Mortgage Rates

NAHB/Wells Fargo Housing Market Index · June 2026

Builder Mood Stuck Near Foreclosure-Crisis Lows Even as Mortgage Rates Ease

Builder confidence fell two points to 35 in June, according to the NAHB/Wells Fargo Housing Market Index, missing the consensus forecast of 36 and marking the 26th straight month the gauge has sat below the 50-point breakeven line — a stretch of pessimism the trade group says hasn't been seen since 2011–2012. Current sales conditions fell two points to 38, and traffic of prospective buyers remains a "major pain point," per NAHB officials, even as nearly a third of builders are cutting prices by an average of 6% and 62% are leaning on sales incentives to move inventory.

The one bright spot is financing costs: NerdWallet's daily average put the 30-year fixed mortgage rate at 6.29% Wednesday morning, down three basis points and now 13 basis points below where it sat a week ago. Freddie Mac's weekly survey, last updated June 11, had the 30-year averaging 6.52%, still nearly half a point below this time last year. Whether today's Fed decision nudges that average further depends heavily on how the dot plot reads — a hawkish surprise could quickly erase the week's modest gains.

Armstrong Economics

Capital Flows & the War Cycle

Armstrong's Latest: Sovereign Debt Stress Is the Real Story Behind Every "Brushfire" War

Martin Armstrong's recent commentary keeps returning to the same throughline: scattered geopolitical flare-ups, from the Iran conflict to renewed talk of a Taiwan flashpoint later this year, are symptoms of a deeper sovereign debt unwind rather than standalone crises. His capital-flow model continues to track institutional money rotating out of confidence in government debt broadly, which he argues explains why stocks, gold, and the dollar have at times risen together — a pattern he says reflects capital fleeing Europe rather than a simple flight to a single safe haven. Armstrong has reiterated his multi-year bullish gold call, with resistance eyed near $8,500 an ounce and a longer-term target near $10,000, alongside a silver range of roughly $165 to $200.

The more pointed warning in his recent media appearances is about capital controls rather than war itself: Spain's recent cap on withdrawals without government approval is the kind of policy Armstrong has flagged as a precursor to broader, digital-currency-enabled controls elsewhere. With Warsh's debut rate decision landing the same week as the Iran deal's formal signing, Armstrong's Economic Confidence Model continues to flag the second half of 2026 as a pivotal stretch for distinguishing genuine de-escalation from what he's previously called a temporary lull before the next leg of his broader war cycle.

Health, Wellness & Fitness

Tea Drinkers May Get a Longevity Boost — But the Preparation Matters

A major review tied regular tea consumption to lower risk of heart disease, diabetes, cancer, and age-related cognitive and muscle decline, but researchers flagged an important caveat: bottled and bubble teas often carry added sugars and ingredients that can cancel out the benefit, meaning the way a person drinks their tea matters nearly as much as whether they drink it at all.

Largest-Ever Opioid Review Finds Limited Benefit for Acute Pain

The most comprehensive review yet conducted on opioids for acute pain concluded that these widely prescribed drugs often deliver only modest, short-lived relief — adding to a growing body of evidence prompting clinicians to reconsider how routinely opioids are reached for after injuries or minor procedures.

Poor Sleep Breathing Patterns Linked to Faster Cardiovascular Aging

New research connects disrupted nighttime breathing, including obstructive sleep apnea, to accelerated cardiovascular aging and a higher risk of premature death, with circadian rhythm disruption emerging as a likely mechanism. The findings add sleep quality to the growing list of modifiable factors, alongside diet and exercise, that meaningfully shape long-term heart health.
