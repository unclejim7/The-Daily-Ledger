World & Geopolitics

Second Night of US Strikes on Iran — Strait of Hormuz Closed, Markets Rebound on Pause Signal

Trump warns of more hits tonight; Iran shuts the world's most vital oil chokepoint; futures recover as US military says latest strikes are done.

The United States launched a second consecutive night of airstrikes against targets across Iran on Wednesday into Thursday, with US Central Command confirming strikes on military sites after President Trump declared on social media that Iran had "taken too long to negotiate a deal that would have been great for them, now they will have to pay the price." Iran responded by formally closing the Strait of Hormuz to all marine traffic — the waterway through which roughly 20% of global oil supply once flowed — and launching retaliatory drone strikes on Bahrain, Kuwait and Jordan. US stock futures rebounded 0.4%–0.7% Thursday morning after the Defense Department announced the latest strike wave had concluded, raising hopes for a brief tactical pause as back-channel negotiations continue. Trump simultaneously warned the military could again strike critical Iranian infrastructure "very hard tonight," keeping markets on edge. Defense Secretary Hegseth framed the strikes as aimed at strengthening America's negotiating position. The ECB raised rates on Thursday specifically to fight Iran-war inflation, underscoring the conflict's global economic reach.

Diplomacy

Peace Talks Stall as Iran Doubles Down on Hormuz

US-Iran negotiations remain stuck over Iran's insistence on maintaining its chokehold on the Strait of Hormuz as leverage. Trump has separately floated taking "total control" of Iran's oil industry and capturing the energy hub of Kharg Island "at some point." No ceasefire framework has been announced.

Energy Markets

Oil Prices: Rebound From Lows on Strike-Pause Hopes

Crude prices swung sharply in both directions overnight. The Strait closure — if sustained — would affect nearly a fifth of global oil supply. Energy prices drove more than 60% of May's CPI monthly increase, with gasoline +40.5% year over year. Analysts warn food price spikes are still ahead.

Macroeconomics & Federal Reserve
CPI Report

Inflation Hits 4.2% — Three-Year High

May CPI rose 0.5% month-over-month and 4.2% year-over-year, the highest since April 2023. Energy prices jumped 3.9% in the month and are up 23.5% over the year. Core CPI came in at 2.9% annually — slightly cooler than feared. Gasoline surged 40.5% year-over-year.

Federal Reserve

Warsh Faces His First FOMC — June 16–17

Kevin Warsh, sworn in as the 17th Fed Chair on May 22, chairs his first FOMC meeting in five days. Rates remain at 3.50–3.75% after three consecutive holds. JPMorgan expects the Fed to hold through year-end but shift explicitly to a neutral bias, dropping the prior easing lean. Bond markets now price a rate hike as possible by October.

Bond Market Signal

Treasury Market to Warsh: Rates Aren't High Enough

The 2-year Treasury yield at ~4.15% sits well above the Fed's 3.50–3.75% policy band — a divergence that began in March. Bloomberg's Robert Burgess wrote that Trump's unofficial mandate for Warsh was to cut rates, but "the economy hasn't cooperated." A hike now looks more plausible than a cut.

Bitcoin & Digital Assets

BTC: $63,021 — War & Fed Anxiety Pin Crypto to Range

Bitcoin opened at $61,456 Thursday before recovering to ~$63,021 by 7:39 AM ET, up from recent lows as US strike-pause news lifted risk appetite. Ethereum opened at $1,620 and firmed to $1,660. The macro overhang — Iran war uncertainty plus a hawkish Fed repricing — continues to suppress crypto momentum. Bitcoin remains roughly 38% below its October 2025 all-time high of $126,198. BlackRock's IBIT and Fidelity's FBTC are consolidating their dominance of spot Bitcoin ETF flows, leaving smaller funds increasingly sidelined. The CLARITY Act enters a critical phase: Senate Banking Committee advanced it 15-9 on May 14, and a July 4 signing target was floated by White House digital asset adviser Patrick Witt. Polymarket prices a 72% chance of passage in 2026. Analysts say BTC's base case on CLARITY passage is $95K–$130K over 12 months.

30-Yr Fixed: 6.48% (June 4 Survey)

The most recent Freddie Mac Primary Mortgage Market Survey (June 4) put the 30-year fixed rate at 6.48%, down from 6.53% the prior week. The 15-year averaged 5.79%. A year ago the 30-year was 6.85%. Oxford Economics' Nancy Vanden Houten says May may mark the CPI peak, but "inflation will be slow to decline." The new Freddie Mac PMMS releases Thursday — check for any weekly shift.

Health, Wellness & Fitness
Research

GLP-1 Drugs Linked to 30% Lower Breast Cancer Risk

A large study found women taking GLP-1 receptor agonists — the drug class behind Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro, and Zepbound — were about 30% less likely to develop breast cancer. Researchers say findings are promising but not yet proof. Clinical trials are now being planned to test whether these drugs could help prevent breast cancer.

Nutrition

Ultra-Processed Foods Linked to Slower Cognition

A study of more than 2,100 adults found that eating more ultra-processed foods was associated with poorer attention and slower mental processing — even among people with otherwise healthy diets. Researchers flagged that higher consumption was also linked to greater anxiety and cognitive fatigue.

Sleep Science

Sleep Apnea's Heart Risk Traced to the Gut

A new study found a surprising gut-heart connection explaining why sleep apnea increases cardiovascular disease risk. In mice, disabling a bile acid receptor called FXR sharply reduced arterial plaque buildup, opening potential new treatment pathways beyond CPAP and weight loss for the roughly 1 billion people worldwide with sleep apnea.

Fact of the Day
The Strait of Hormuz Is Just 21 Miles Wide
At its narrowest point, the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly 20% of the world's oil once flowed daily — measures only 21 nautical miles across. Two shipping lanes of 2 miles each are separated by a 2-mile buffer zone.
Word of the Day
Derecho
From the Spanish for "straight" or "direct." A derecho is a widespread, long-lived wind storm associated with a fast-moving band of severe thunderstorms. Unlike a tornado, it moves in a straight line — and it doesn't apologize.
