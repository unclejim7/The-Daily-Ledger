Markets · Technology · Thursday Edition

Micron Delivers Its Strongest Quarter in History — $41.46B Revenue, $50B Q4 Guidance, and a 17% Afterhours Pop Rescue the AI Rally

Micron Technology turned what had been shaping up as a disaster week for AI stocks into a euphoric Thursday morning. Reporting after Wednesday’s close, the memory giant posted fiscal Q3 2026 results that shattered every metric the Street had penciled in: revenue of $41.46 billion against consensus expectations of $35.84 billion, non-GAAP EPS of $25.11 versus the $20.28 forecast, and gross margins of 84.9% — well above the 81% guidance the company had issued heading into the quarter. The year-over-year comparison was breathtaking: Micron earned $1.68 per share on $9.3 billion in revenue in the same quarter last year. Thursday morning, the stock surged roughly 17% in premarket trading, landing near $1,225 per share and dragging the entire semiconductor complex higher with it. Qualcomm added 9% on its own positive catalyst — raising its 2029 non-handset revenue target to $40 billion from $22 billion. Sandisk, Western Digital, Lam Research, KLA, and Applied Materials all rose in sympathy.

The forward guidance was the true market-mover. Micron projected fiscal Q4 revenue of $50 billion — up from $11.3 billion in the prior year period and $7 billion above what analysts had modeled. EPS guidance for Q4 was set at $31 per share, plus or minus $1. The company announced it has signed 17 Strategic Customer Agreements providing guaranteed revenue and visibility, expects to raise fiscal 2026 capital spending to $27 billion, and committed to quarterly capex in fiscal 2027 above Q4 levels. Gross margin guidance for Q4 was set at approximately 86%, which would be a new record. CFO Mark Murphy described the SCA structure as giving Micron committed volume against which it can confidently scale investment. CEO Sanjay Mehrotra closed with the line the market had been waiting for: “AI has elevated the value of memory.”

The relief rally lit up the broader market. Nasdaq futures climbed nearly 2% Wednesday night and the S&P 500 opened Thursday up roughly 0.8%, with the Dow adding more than 300 points. The Micron print answered the core question the AI trade needed resolved after Tuesday’s KOSPI-driven meltdown and Wednesday’s continued drift: demand for HBM and high-capacity DRAM from hyperscalers is not slowing, it is accelerating. Cloud Memory Business Unit revenue alone came in at $13.77 billion in Q3 — up more than 300% year-over-year. European chip stocks followed suit, with ASMI, BE Semiconductor, and Soitec all posting sharp gains in overseas trading.

The macro backdrop Thursday morning was more complex than the chip euphoria suggested. May PCE — the Fed’s preferred inflation measure — printed at 4.1% year-over-year, the highest level since April 2023, exactly in line with consensus but confirming that inflation peaked in May amid the Iran conflict’s energy price spike. Core PCE came in at 3.4%, also in line. The PCE print did not derail the rally: markets interpreted the in-line reading as confirmation that May was indeed the inflation peak, with oil prices already down sharply in June as Hormuz traffic resumes. Initial jobless claims fell to 215,000 for the week ended June 20, down 12,000 from the prior reading. The combination — Micron euphoria plus a no-surprise PCE print plus improving labor data — gave markets everything they needed to stage a broad Thursday recovery.

Micron Beat

$41.46B Revenue and $25.11 EPS — Strongest Quarter in Company History by Every Measure

Micron’s fiscal Q3 2026 exceeded every dimension of consensus. Revenue of $41.46 billion came in nearly $6 billion above the $35.84 billion estimate. Non-GAAP EPS of $25.11 beat by $4.62. Gross margin reached 84.9%, up from 74.9% the prior quarter and 39% a year ago. Cloud Memory Business Unit revenue hit $13.77 billion, up more than 300% year-over-year, with segment operating margins at 78%. The company closed the quarter with $30.2 billion in cash and marketable investments. Management flagged that 17 Strategic Customer Agreements now provide committed volume and revenue visibility heading into fiscal 2027 — a structural underpinning that management said justifies accelerating capex well into next fiscal year.

Micron Q4 Guidance

$50B Revenue, $31 EPS, 86% Gross Margin — Q4 Would Be Another Record Quarter by a Wide Margin

Micron guided fiscal Q4 2026 for revenue of $50 billion (plus or minus $1 billion), EPS of $31 (plus or minus $1), and gross margin of approximately 86% — each a company record by a significant margin. Full-year fiscal 2026 capex is now expected at approximately $27 billion; quarterly capex in fiscal 2027 will rise above Q4 levels, with more than half of the year-over-year increase from construction as new cleanroom capacity comes online across Idaho, New York, Virginia, Taiwan, Singapore, and Japan. Management also committed to returning 100% of excess cash to shareholders over time, with increased capital return beginning December 9, 2026 — the second anniversary of Micron’s CHIPS Act definitive agreements.

PCE — May 2026

PCE Hits 4.1% — Three-Year High and the Fed’s Most Challenging Reading Yet, But Markets Expect This Was the Peak

The Commerce Department reported Thursday that the Personal Consumption Expenditures price index rose at a 4.1% annual rate in May — the highest level since April 2023 and a jump from April’s 3.8%. The monthly gain was 0.4%, a tick below the 0.5% forecast. Core PCE, stripping out food and energy, rose 3.4% year-over-year and 0.3% month-over-month — both in line with consensus and the highest annual core reading since October 2023. Personal income rose 0.7%, well above the 0.4% expected. The saving rate climbed to 3%. The critical context: May’s reading was driven primarily by the oil price spike during the Iran conflict, and crude has since fallen sharply as Hormuz traffic resumes. Analysts at UBS, BofA, and Goldman broadly expect June PCE to decline notably, which would remove the Fed’s most pressing hike rationale.

JPMorgan Leadership

Dimon Names Doug Petno and Troy Rohrbaugh Co-Presidents — Marianne Lake to Retire After 25 Years

JPMorgan Chase named Doug Petno and Troy Rohrbaugh co-presidents effective immediately, the bank disclosed Thursday in a regulatory filing. The two executives jointly led the commercial and investment banking division since 2024 and will now take on broader roles: Petno as CEO of commercial and investment banking, Rohrbaugh leading consumer and community banking, replacing Marianne Lake, a 25-year JPMorgan veteran who served as CFO starting in 2013 and was long considered a top Dimon succession candidate. CEO Jamie Dimon said the changes reflect the board’s confidence in both executives’ performance. The move advances the most visible succession planning process at any major U.S. bank.

Federal Reserve & Inflation

PCE Print — Rate Path Reaction

May PCE at 4.1% Meets Forecasts, Core In-Line — Warsh Fed Now Watching June Data for Hike Decision

Thursday’s May PCE report arrived exactly where Wall Street expected, which in the current environment counted as mild relief. The 4.1% headline print confirmed the inflation surge driven by Iran-related oil prices, but the in-line result meant the Fed received no fresh hawkish surprise on top of what was already priced. Core PCE at 3.4% — the highest annual reading since October 2023 — remains well above the Fed’s 2% target and gives Chair Kevin Warsh ample justification to stay on hold or hike. However, the critical variable is the forward data path: crude oil is now at $69.47, down from nearly $97 at the Hormuz blockade peak, and that deflationary energy impulse will flow through into June and July PCE readings. Chris Zaccarelli, CIO at Northlight Asset Management, framed it precisely: June PCE needs to print lower than May or the pressure for a September hike will intensify. Deutsche Bank’s base case remains two hikes in 2026, bringing the federal funds rate to 4.1%; BofA holds the more aggressive three-hike call for September, October, and December. The July 28–29 FOMC meeting is not yet live for a hike, but the language there will be closely watched.

Health, Wellness & Fitness

After a Brutal Week, the Rally Psychology: Why a Market Reversal Feels Like Physical Relief — and the Trap It Sets

The body doesn’t distinguish between abstract financial stress and direct physical threat — both trigger the same cortisol response, the same hypervigilance, the same impaired sleep quality. When Micron’s blowout print hit the wires Wednesday night and futures surged, many investors experienced something that felt like a physiological release: a literal lowering of tension. Research in behavioral finance calls this the “relief rally cognition trap.” After extended stress, the sudden removal of the stressor can produce overconfidence in the rebound: investors who were appropriately cautious at the bottom become irrationally bullish at the first sign of recovery. The evidence-based countermeasure is to treat today’s Micron-driven rally with the same disciplined framework applied to Tuesday’s selloff — portfolio changes made in acute emotional states, whether fear or relief, historically underperform changes made in calm, data-driven conditions. The PCE printed in line. The macro thesis did not change. One blowout earnings report, however spectacular, doesn’t re-anchor a rate cycle.

Random Fact

The term “High Bandwidth Memory” (HBM) was coined by AMD and SK Hynix in 2013 and standardized by JEDEC as a new interface for 3D-stacked DRAM. HBM1, introduced in 2015, transferred data at 128 GB/s. HBM4, the current generation that Micron has been shipping since calendar Q1 2026 for Nvidia’s Vera Rubin platform, transfers data at more than 1.5 terabytes per second per stack — nearly 12 times the bandwidth of HBM1 in roughly a decade. Micron’s $41.46 billion quarter exists because that bandwidth is now the most strategically constrained resource in artificial intelligence infrastructure.

Word of the Day
blowout

(noun/adjective, American English slang, 19th century) — in financial parlance, an earnings result that exceeds analyst consensus expectations by a margin wide enough to produce a large, immediate price move. Originally referred to the rapid release of compressed gas or oil from a well under pressure. Today’s Micron earnings call was a blowout by any definition: revenue exceeded consensus by $5.62 billion, EPS beat by $4.62, and Q4 guidance came in $6.4 billion above what analysts had modeled. Not all blowouts sustain — the question is whether the Q4 guide is beatable, too.
