Breaking · U.S.-Iran War

"The Deal With Iran Is Now Complete": Trump Authorizes Reopening of Strait of Hormuz, Markets Surge to Start the Week

President Trump announced late Sunday that the United States and Iran have reached an agreement to end more than three and a half months of war and reopen the Strait of Hormuz, the chokepoint through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil and gas trade normally flows. "The Deal with the Islamic Republic of Iran is now complete," Trump wrote on Truth Social, authorizing the "toll free opening" of the strait and the immediate removal of the U.S. naval blockade of Iranian ports. "Ships of the World, start your engines. Let the oil flow!"

Pakistani Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif, whose government has served as a key mediator, confirmed a signing ceremony is scheduled for Friday, June 19, in Switzerland — though some reports point to a slightly later date of June 19 amid ongoing negotiation of details. The agreement reportedly establishes a 60-day ceasefire and a memorandum of understanding between Washington and Tehran, with a number of harder issues set aside for further talks over the coming two months. UN Secretary-General António Guterres welcomed the announcement as a "critical step," while G7 leaders — gathering Monday in Evian-les-Bains, France — were set to discuss the strait's long-term reopening and demining, with Britain and France offering to assist.

Markets reacted immediately and emphatically. Dow futures jumped as much as 504 points (+0.98%), Nasdaq futures surged over 2%, and S&P 500 futures rose roughly 1.3%. The VIX collapsed more than 14% to around 16.6. Crude oil plunged over 5% to roughly $80 a barrel — its steepest one-day drop in months — as traders priced in the return of Persian Gulf supply. Gold, ironically, also rose sharply, up nearly 2.8% to north of $4,350, suggesting some investors see the rally as fragile. The war began February 28 with U.S. and Israeli strikes on Iran that included the killing of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei; this is the third ceasefire attempt since, after frameworks in April and a U.S. strike that broke an early-June truce. Caution is warranted — but for now, "Let the oil flow" is the line of the day.

Federal Reserve

FOMC Preview · June 16-17

Warsh's First Meeting Arrives Tomorrow — A Hold Is Priced In, But Tone Is Everything

Kevin Warsh, sworn in May 22 as the 17th chair of the Federal Reserve, presides over his first FOMC meeting Tuesday and Wednesday with May CPI sitting at a three-year high of 4.2% and a sitting president publicly demanding rate cuts. The CME FedWatch tool shows roughly a 97% probability the Fed holds the federal funds rate at 3.50%–3.75%, and a Reuters poll found 72 of 102 economists expect no change through year-end. J.P. Morgan's Phil Camporeale said the more interesting move would be an explicit shift in the policy statement's language — from a bias toward easing to a neutral stance.

The real action will be in Wednesday's updated Summary of Economic Projections and dot plot, plus Warsh's first press conference as chair. Warsh has signaled he favors a leaner Fed that communicates less than under his predecessor, stepping back from some of the detailed forward guidance markets grew used to. With Monday's Iran deal potentially easing the energy-driven inflation pressure that pushed May CPI to 4.2%, Warsh may have a small opening to sound less alarmed than he otherwise would — though one day of oil futures doesn't undo three months of war-driven price pressure.

Recovery Watch

Bitcoin Opens at Highest Level Since Early June — But "Not Fully Out of Danger"

Bitcoin opened Monday at $65,710 — about 2% above Sunday's open and its highest level since the start of June — before climbing further to roughly $66,157 by mid-morning. Spot Bitcoin ETFs logged a combined $85.8 million in net inflows Monday, with iShares' IBIT and Fidelity's FBTC leading the turnaround after weeks of outflows. Ethereum followed a similar pattern, rising to roughly $1,762.

Coverage from CoinDesk cautions that Bitcoin remains "not fully out of danger": both the April ceasefire and the brief truce that collapsed after June 9 U.S. strikes saw Bitcoin give back its entire relief rally within days. Whether Monday's gains hold likely depends on how the week's FOMC decision and the actual signing of the Iran deal play out.

Geopolitics & Current Events

G7 Summit · Evian-les-Bains, France

G7 Leaders to Discuss Hormuz Reopening and Demining as Trump Departs for France

President Trump boarded Air Force One Monday bound for the G7 summit in Evian-les-Bains, France, where French President Emmanuel Macron said leaders would discuss the long-term reopening of the Strait of Hormuz under the new U.S.-Iran agreement. Britain and France have both expressed interest in helping demine the strait, which Iran has effectively controlled — and seeded with mines — since shortly after the war began. The scale of the minefield remains unclear.

Signing Logistics

Formal Signing Set for Friday, June 19, in Switzerland

Pakistan's government, which brokered much of the back-channel diplomacy, says the agreement is scheduled to be formally signed Friday in Switzerland — the same day U.S. markets are closed for Juneteenth. Reports describe the current framework as a 60-day ceasefire and a memorandum of understanding, with additional negotiations over the next two months to resolve issues both sides set aside for now.

Caution Flag

Trump Warns Further Strikes Remain Possible Despite Deal

Even as markets celebrated, reporting Monday morning noted Trump has not entirely ruled out further action against Iran if the agreement falters — a reminder that this is the third attempted de-escalation since February's war began, after frameworks in April and early June both broke down within days. Investors who got burned twice already are, understandably, treating "Let the oil flow" as an opening bid rather than a closing bell.

Armstrong Economics

War Cycle & Gold

Armstrong's War Cycle Thesis Faces Its Biggest Test Yet — And Gold Isn't Buying the All-Clear

Martin Armstrong's long-standing War Cycle model has flagged 2026–2027 as the peak of a roughly 25-year war cycle, with his Economic Confidence Model pointing to a downturn beginning around mid-2026. Armstrong has repeatedly cautioned that prior Iran ceasefires — April's framework and the brief early-June truce — were "temporary lulls" rather than resolutions, a call that proved correct both times when hostilities resumed within days.

Monday's market action offers a real-time test of that thesis from an unusual angle: gold rose nearly 2.8% to over $4,350 even as the broader market rallied hard on peace-deal optimism — an unusual combination, since gold typically falls when geopolitical risk eases. In recent commentary, Armstrong has framed gold's strength as reflecting institutional loss of confidence in sovereign debt rather than a pure war-risk hedge, distinguishing it from Bitcoin, which he continues to view as a speculative trading vehicle rather than a store of value comparable to gold. If gold keeps climbing even as the Iran story de-escalates, it would be consistent with Armstrong's broader argument that the more durable story isn't the war itself, but what years of war-driven deficit spending have done to confidence in government debt.

Health, Wellness & Fitness

How You Drink Tea Matters for Longevity, Major Review Finds

A large new review published this month found that tea can meaningfully protect against heart disease, diabetes, cancer, cognitive decline, and age-related muscle loss — but the benefits depend heavily on how it's consumed. Bottled and bubble teas, often loaded with added sugars and other ingredients, can substantially diminish or even reverse the protective effects seen with plain brewed tea. For Daily Ledger readers stress-testing their routines ahead of a busy news week, the takeaway is simple: the tea itself isn't the variable, what you put in the cup is.

World's Largest Opioid Review: Often Only Small, Short-Lived Benefits for Acute Pain

The largest review ever conducted on opioid medications for acute pain concluded that, for many common conditions including some surgical recoveries, opioids deliver only modest and short-lived relief compared to alternatives — reinforcing years of clinical guidance pushing toward non-opioid pain management as a first-line approach where appropriate. The findings don't apply to every situation, but add to a substantial body of evidence prompting many providers to default to other options first.
