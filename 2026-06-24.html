All Eyes on Micron Tonight: Markets Attempt a Bounce After the AI Rout, But the HBM Number Will Decide Everything

Wall Street is trying to find its footing Wednesday morning, with S&P 500 futures up 0.32%, Nasdaq futures climbing 0.43%, and the Dow holding near flat after Tuesday’s brutal session delivered the deepest two-day Nasdaq decline since the Spring 2025 chip wreck. Tuesday’s final numbers were unambiguous: the S&P 500 shed 1.44% to close at 7,365.46, the Nasdaq dropped 2.21% to 25,587.04, and the Dow managed to hang on, losing just 45.87 points to end at 51,666.84. The selling was concentrated in semiconductors and AI infrastructure names — Micron itself fell more than 13% on Tuesday as KOSPI contagion swept through memory names. Micron shares had already pulled back considerably heading into the print. Now they have to deliver.

The report is due tonight at 4:30 PM ET, with a post-earnings analyst call at 6:00 PM. The Street consensus calls for revenue of roughly $34.5 billion and adjusted EPS of approximately $19.72 — numbers that imply 271% year-over-year revenue growth and 932% EPS expansion from the same quarter last year when Micron earned $1.91 on $9.3 billion in sales. The company itself guided for $33.5 billion ± $750 million in revenue and non-GAAP EPS of $19.15 ± $0.40, with gross margins at approximately 81% — the highest in the firm’s history. Micron has crossed the $1 trillion market cap threshold this year and is up roughly 244% year-to-date even after Tuesday’s selloff. The bull case is straightforward: HBM supply for all of 2026 is sold out, giving the company rare visibility into a market where pricing power is structural rather than cyclical.

What the market will actually trade on tonight is forward guidance: specifically, whether HBM revenue is accelerating from the $1 billion-plus reached in Q2, whether Micron raises its FY2027 capex posture, and whether management signals any softening in DRAM or NAND pricing. Goldman Sachs kept a neutral rating with a revised price target of $900 while noting peak earnings may come in fiscal 2027 at $138.86 per share. BTIG initiated with a buy and a $290 target, noting the company is “firing on all cylinders.” A beat and strong forward guidance tonight could stabilize not just Micron but SanDisk, Western Digital, and the Nasdaq broadly. A miss or cautious HBM language could extend Tuesday’s rout into a third consecutive week of tech underperformance.

Meanwhile, two other events pulled focus Wednesday: S&P Global announced that Alphabet (GOOG) will join the Dow Jones Industrial Average next Monday, replacing an undisclosed component, capping a remarkable run for the search and AI giant even as its shares fell 5% Monday on talent departure concerns. And BofA Global Research and Deutsche Bank formally published forecasts calling for three Fed rate hikes in 2026 — September, October, and December — under Chair Kevin Warsh, a more hawkish call than the market had been pricing and one that kept the 10-year Treasury yield elevated. The 2-year yield hit its highest point since February 2025 on Tuesday per TheStreet data. PCE inflation on Friday remains the single most important near-term data point for the rate outlook and for whether the July 28–29 FOMC meeting becomes contentious.

Health, Wellness & Fitness

What a Two-Day Market Rout Actually Does to Your Stress Physiology — and How to Counter It

The past 48 hours — a KOSPI crash, a tech rout, BofA calling for three rate hikes, Micron falling 13% in a single session — constitute the kind of high-density negative news environment that research consistently shows elevates cortisol and activates sustained threat-monitoring in the brain. The problem isn’t acute stress, which the body handles efficiently. It’s the drip: a constant background hum of financial uncertainty that impairs sleep, decision quality, and working memory. Two countermeasures with solid evidence: time-restricted information consumption (checking markets twice a day rather than continuously) measurably reduces the cortisol arc over a week, per behavioral finance studies. And distinguishing between “information” (Micron’s HBM guidance tonight will matter; today’s premarket noise will not) and “noise” reduces the cognitive load that makes a red market day feel more threatening than it is. Micron’s print tonight is information. Everything between now and 4:30 PM ET is largely noise.

Random Fact

Micron Technology was founded in 1978 in a dentist’s office in Boise, Idaho, by Ward Parkinson, Joe Parkinson, Dennis Wilson, and Doug Pitman — with $10,000 in initial funding. The company went public in 1984 at $14 per share. As of Micron’s pre-selloff peak this month, those original shares had multiplied roughly 86,000-fold in value — one of the longest compounded returns in semiconductor history. Tonight’s earnings report, expected to show $34.5 billion in quarterly revenue, would have been incomprehensible to the founders who started in borrowed dental office space 48 years ago.

Word of the Day
guidance

(noun, from Old French: guider, “to guide”) — in financial parlance, a company’s forward-looking projection of expected future revenue, earnings, or other financial metrics, issued to help investors calibrate expectations for upcoming periods. Unlike historical results, guidance is inherently uncertain and carries legal implications under securities law; firms can be sued if guidance is materially misleading. Tonight, Micron’s fiscal Q4 guidance will arguably matter more than its Q3 actuals — a reminder that in markets, the future always outweighs the past.
