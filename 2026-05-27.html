Markets & Finance

Markets
Dow Hits Record as Warsh Nomination Calms Markets — Chips Stumble on Export Controls
The Dow Jones Industrial Average notched a record close Tuesday, climbing 282 points as investors welcomed the nomination of Kevin Warsh as Federal Reserve Chair. Warsh, a former Fed governor known for market-friendly hawkishness, is seen as a credible inflation fighter who won't upend the current rate trajectory. However, the tech-heavy Nasdaq slid as Qualcomm plunged nearly 10% after new export control restrictions on chip sales to China rattled the semiconductor sector. The split session underscores the two narratives competing for market attention: AI infrastructure euphoria versus geopolitical friction in the supply chain.
Federal Reserve

Warsh Nominated — What It Means for Rates

Kevin Warsh is a known hawk who dissented in favor of tighter policy during his prior Fed tenure. His nomination signals the administration wants credible anti-inflation leadership. Markets are pricing a hold at the June meeting and watching for signals on the path through year-end.

Geopolitics
Iran Conflict: 60 Days In, Diplomacy Opens a Window
Two months after the U.S. launched strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities in response to a sustained missile campaign, the two nations have opened a diplomatic back-channel in Muscat, Oman. The preliminary talks represent the first direct engagement since the conflict began and follow quiet mediation by the Sultanate of Oman. Neither side has committed to a formal ceasefire framework yet. Tehran is demanding a full halt to U.S. operations; Washington is insisting on verification mechanisms for any nuclear freeze. The Strait of Hormuz has remained technically open throughout, though commercial shipping has significantly rerouted to avoid the Persian Gulf approach corridor.

Spring Market "Weird" — Listings Outpace Buyers

For the first time since the pandemic era began, active listings are outpacing new buyer contracts nationally. Sellers are listing but buyers are hesitating at 6.85% mortgage rates. The result: homes sitting longer, modest price reductions appearing in previously bulletproof suburban markets.

Tax & Accounting

OBBBA and SALT: What Illinois Taxpayers Need to Know Now

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act's SALT deduction cap expansion is the most consequential provision for high-property-tax states. Illinois taxpayers — particularly in Cook County and Lake County where property tax bills regularly exceed $15,000–$20,000 — stand to benefit significantly if the $40,000 cap clears the Senate. Current law caps the deduction at $10,000. Tax advisors are recommending clients model both scenarios before making any decisions on property purchases, accelerated deductions, or entity structuring. The bill's fate in the Senate is genuinely uncertain; Senator Thune has signaled he wants modifications before a floor vote.

Armstrong Economics

Armstrong: Capital Flight Into U.S. Equities Driving the Bull — Not the Fed

Martin Armstrong's long-standing thesis is that the current U.S. equity bull market is being driven primarily by international capital flight rather than Federal Reserve policy. Europe's fiscal dysfunction, China's property crisis, and now the Iran military conflict have all accelerated flows into dollar-denominated assets. Armstrong's Socrates model has identified May–June 2026 as a key cyclical inflection point. He expects volatility to increase heading into the summer as the Iran situation resolves — one way or the other — and global capital recalibrates its positioning. His baseline outlook remains bullish for U.S. equities through late 2027, with the caveat that sovereign debt markets pose the primary systemic risk.

Random Fact

Chicago Has More Drawbridges Than Any City on Earth

Chicago operates 37 movable bridges — more than any other city in the world. Most span the Chicago River, which was famously reversed in 1900 to protect the city's drinking water from sewage. The bridges are raised several times daily during the navigation season.

Word of the Day

Hawkish

In monetary policy, a "hawk" favors higher interest rates to combat inflation, even at the cost of slower growth. The opposite — a "dove" — prefers lower rates to stimulate the economy. Kevin Warsh is firmly in the hawk camp, which is why markets reacted positively to his nomination: he's credible on inflation.
