Markets
S&P 500 Crosses 7,600 as Iran Ceasefire Wobbles and Chip Stocks Surge
The S&P 500 closed above 7,600 for the first time Tuesday — the latest milestone in a run that has produced nine consecutive weekly gains. Hewlett Packard Enterprise surged 19% on AI chip news from the Computex Taipei conference, dragging broader technology higher. The Dow added 0.45% while the Nasdaq barely edged up 0.03%. But the backdrop remained unsettled. Semiofficial Iranian news agencies reported that Tehran had suspended contact with ceasefire mediators in protest of Israeli military operations in Lebanon and Gaza, sending oil higher and introducing a new layer of geopolitical uncertainty. The market largely shrugged off the Iran headline — for now — choosing instead to focus on the AI hardware story and the upcoming jobs report Friday.

Current Events

Geopolitics
Iran Pulls Back from Ceasefire Table — Citing Israeli Actions in Lebanon and Gaza
Iran's suspension of contact with U.S.-backed ceasefire mediators Tuesday marked the most significant setback in the MOU process since talks began in Muscat six weeks ago. Tehran cited ongoing Israeli military operations in Lebanon and Gaza as incompatible with the spirit of negotiations — a linkage the U.S. has consistently rejected, arguing the two conflicts are separate. The development does not mean the talks are dead: Iranian officials left open the possibility of resuming contact once "conditions change." But it resets the clock on the MOU timeline and reintroduces the oil shock scenario that markets had largely priced out. WTI crude jumped $2 on the news. U.S. officials expressed frustration privately while publicly calling for Iran to return to the table.
Congress

Senate Finance Committee Marks Up OBBBA — SALT Takes Shape

The Senate Finance Committee concluded two days of markup sessions on the One Big Beautiful Bill Act. The SALT deduction cap survived but was trimmed: the Senate version phases in the $40,000 cap over three years rather than immediately. The 199A passthrough extension and PMI deductibility restoration both survived markup largely intact.

Tech / Policy

EU AI Act Compliance Costs Hit $4B for U.S. Tech Sector

Industry estimates released Tuesday put the first-year compliance cost of the EU AI Act at approximately $4 billion for U.S. technology companies with European operations. The costs cover documentation, auditing, model cards, and human oversight infrastructure. Several smaller AI startups are reportedly considering pulling EU operations entirely rather than bearing the compliance burden.

Tax & Accounting

Senate SALT Changes: What the Phase-In Means for Illinois Filers

The Senate Finance Committee's decision to phase in the SALT cap increase over three years — rather than the House's immediate jump to $40,000 — is meaningful for Illinois taxpayers. Under the Senate version, the cap would rise from $10,000 to approximately $20,000 in 2026, $30,000 in 2027, and $40,000 in 2028. For a Chicagoland homeowner paying $18,000 in annual property taxes, the 2026 benefit would be roughly $800–$1,200 in federal tax savings (depending on marginal rate) — meaningful but not the full relief the House version promised. Tax advisors should update their modeling to reflect the Senate version as the more likely outcome, while noting that the House-Senate conference process could still change the final number.

Armstrong Economics

Armstrong: Iran Setback Is a Delay, Not a Reversal — Capital Flows Unchanged

Martin Armstrong's framework would treat Tuesday's Iran mediator suspension as a cyclical delay rather than a structural reversal. His Socrates model does not rely on the Iran deal as a prerequisite for the equity bull thesis — the capital flight dynamic driving U.S. asset prices operates independently of any single geopolitical event. Armstrong has consistently argued that the structural problems in European sovereign debt, Chinese property markets, and emerging market instability are the real drivers of dollar-denominated asset inflows. Iran resolving or not resolving changes the oil price and the risk-premium on energy, but does not alter the fundamental capital allocation shift that is sustaining the U.S. equity bull. His September–October watch window remains unchanged.

Health & Wellness

Longevity

AI Study: Thymus Health May Predict Lifespan

A Mass General Brigham AI study analyzing imaging data from 50,000 patients found that thymus size and activity — measurable on chest CT scans — was one of the strongest predictors of biological age and remaining lifespan. The thymus, which shrinks with age, appears to be a master regulator of immune system health; people with larger, more active thymus glands at 60 showed immune profiles more typical of 45-year-olds.

Nutrition / Immunity

Methionine Restriction Boosts Infection Survival — Salk Institute

Researchers at the Salk Institute found that temporarily restricting dietary methionine — an amino acid found primarily in meat and eggs — significantly improved survival rates from bacterial infections in animal models. The mechanism involves a metabolic shift that starves pathogens of a key building block while preserving host cell function. Human trials are in early planning stages.

Editor's Dispatch

7,600 and Complications

The S&P 500 crossed 7,600 Tuesday. That's a number worth pausing on — barely six months ago, 7,000 felt like a stretch. Now 7,600 is just another Wednesday milestone. HPE up 19% on chip news from a tech conference in Taipei. The AI trade is not narrowing; it's broadening with every earnings season and every trade show. But Iran pulled back from the ceasefire table, citing Lebanon and Gaza. Oil jumped. The MOU timeline reset. The market barely blinked. That confidence is either wisdom or complacency — and the jobs report Friday will give us a data point on which one it is. If payrolls come in weak, the Iran setback will matter more. If they come in strong, the bull case rolls on. Two days away. Stay tuned.

Random Fact

Chicago's Water Intake Cribs Extend Two Miles Into Lake Michigan

Chicago draws its drinking water from Lake Michigan through two intake cribs located nearly two miles offshore. The cribs were built in the 1860s and remain operational today. The city's water system serves over 5 million people — one of the largest municipal water systems in the world — and the lake provides an essentially limitless freshwater source, which is increasingly recognized as a strategic long-term asset.

Word of the Day

Geopolitical Risk Premium

The extra return investors demand — or extra price consumers pay — to compensate for uncertainty caused by political conflict or instability. Iran's suspension of mediator contact immediately rebuilt the geopolitical risk premium in oil markets that had been fading as deal optimism grew. Premium goes up when conflict risk rises; it compresses when diplomacy advances.

Quote of the Day

— Albert Einstein. The Iran setback is a difficulty. The opportunity is that oil is cheap relative to where it would be if the conflict escalates — and anyone who believes the deal eventually gets done is looking at discounted energy assets right now.
