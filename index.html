Breaking · Iran-U.S. War
A Deal Signed in Pencil: 60-Day Ceasefire Extension Awaits Trump's Pen

The United States and Iran have reached a preliminary agreement to extend their fragile ceasefire by 60 days and open formal nuclear negotiations — but the deal remains in limbo as President Trump has yet to sign off. White House sources confirmed to Al Jazeera and Bloomberg that the framework has been agreed upon; Iran's top negotiator Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf issued a pointed warning that "only actions are the measure" as talks drag on.

Meanwhile, Vice President JD Vance told reporters the sides are "not there yet" on a final agreement, even as he added "we're very close." Markets responded cautiously — equities nudged higher on the news, but oil benchmarks slipped as traders assessed whether the Strait of Hormuz could reopen in any meaningful timeframe.

"We have no trust in guarantees or words — only actions are the measure."

— Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf, Iran's chief negotiator, May 29, 2026
Context · Iran War
Seven Weeks of Ceasefire — and Counting

The original two-week U.S.-Iran ceasefire was brokered by Pakistan on April 8, following 40 days of U.S.-Israeli strikes on Iranian territory. Despite multiple violations and escalations — including Iranian drone launches and U.S. strikes on launch sites near the Strait of Hormuz as recently as Thursday — the truce has held on paper. The proposed 60-day extension would move both sides toward a structured negotiating framework built on Iran's 10-point peace proposal.

🏦 Macroeconomics & Federal Reserve
Fed Watch · Kevin Warsh
Warsh's Hawkish Shadow Lengthens Over the Bond Market

New Fed Chair Kevin Warsh — confirmed by the Senate this month to succeed Jerome Powell — is proving to be exactly what bond markets feared: a monetary realist who won't be rushed into cuts. Warsh has declined to make any promises to the White House on rate reductions, and analysts note it's "no coincidence" that yields have spiked since his confirmation. The 30-year yield above 5% and the 10-year pushing 4.6% represent an effective tightening even without touching the federal funds rate.

Ed Yardeni of Yardeni Research notes the 2-year yield is now above the Fed funds rate — a bond-market signal that traders believe the FFR itself isn't high enough to tame inflation. The June FOMC meeting looms large; markets will parse every word.

Inflation · April 2026 PCE
PCE Hits 3.8% — Highest Since 2023 — as Oil Shock Bites

Thursday's Commerce Department release showed the PCE price index — the Fed's preferred inflation gauge — rising to 3.8% year-over-year in April, up from 3.5% in March, driven by war-related oil price shocks. Core PCE came in at +0.2% month-over-month (a slight cooldown from March's 0.3%), but the year-over-year core rate ticked up to 3.3% — its highest since 2023.

The headline spending number looked resilient (+0.5% nominal), but after inflation, real spending rose just 0.1%. Real per capita disposable income fell 1.4% YOY — the second consecutive negative reading. Americans are drawing down savings at the fastest rate since 2022 to absorb fuel price shock.

"Long-end rates are now in control of monetary policy. I wish Kevin Warsh the best — but he will still be subject to his surrounding macro circumstances."

— Bond market analyst, May 2026

Analysis
Spring Selling Season Arrives Weird: Listings Beat Sales for First Time in 2026

Fannie Mae's May Housing Forecast maintains its 30-year rate projection at 6.3% for Q2 2026, but surging Treasury yields — the Warsh Effect — could push that estimate higher. The spring homebuying season has been "weird," per Money.com: April was the first month this year where annual listings outpaced sales. Half of the nation's 50 largest metros are seeing year-over-year price declines.

The affordability math remains painful. At 6.56% on a $417,700 home with 20% down, the monthly P&I payment runs $2,125 — about 24% of the typical family's monthly income. Chicago buyers fare better than coastal markets, but the thinning margins are universal.

📋 Tax & Accounting
Tax & Policy Watch
Budget Surplus in April — But the Fine Print Is Ugly

The federal government recorded a $215 billion surplus in April — typical for the month when tax collections peak — but the number came in 17% below April 2025. More worrying: interest costs on the national debt hit $97 billion for the month, the second-highest expenditure category after Social Security. As Treasury yields continue to rise under Warsh's watch, the compounding cost of debt service will put growing pressure on fiscal policy and, by extension, future tax legislation.

For individuals and small businesses, the practical takeaway is straightforward: a hawkish Fed keeping rates "higher for longer" means the cost of deferred tax payments and business debt service both rise. Planning your Q2 estimated payments against a backdrop of stubborn inflation (PCE at 3.8%) and elevated borrowing costs deserves careful attention.

Long COVID Brain Inflammation Study: No Widespread Evidence Found
A new brain imaging study found no evidence of widespread neuroinflammation in long COVID patients — challenging earlier hypotheses about the condition's mechanism. Instead, the study points researchers toward other explanations for persistent symptoms, including autonomic nervous system dysfunction and microclotting.
