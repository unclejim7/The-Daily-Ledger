Records Shatter, Then Retreat: Wall Street's Best Week Gives Way to Selloff as Iran Drones Kuwait, Broadcom Misses

MARKETS & GEOPOLITICS DESK — THURSDAY, JUNE 4, 2026

After nine consecutive weeks of record closes, Wall Street's momentum broke hard on Wednesday. The S&P 500 shed 0.74%, the Dow fell 1.21%, and the Nasdaq dropped 0.89% — dragged down by a trifecta of bad news: Iranian drones struck Kuwait International Airport, killing one and sending oil surging toward $100 a barrel; Broadcom tumbled 14% after hours after its Q2 outlook narrowly missed revenue estimates despite strong AI chip revenue of $10.8 billion; and ceasefire negotiations between the U.S. and Iran remain in organized limbo.

The broader equity retreat is a reset, not a rout — the S&P remains near all-time highs, and the prior session had pushed it above 7,600 for the first time ever. But the cracks are visible: oil's persistent elevation above $95 is stoking inflation fears, the Fed is 12 days from its first meeting under Chairman Kevin Warsh, and the AI trade is being tested as even the crown jewel stocks stumble.

Federal Reserve

Warsh's Debut: June 16–17 FOMC — The Statement Matters More Than the Decision

Markets expect a hold at 3.50–3.75% with near-certainty — CME FedWatch was at 65% probability of no change at last check, and Polymarket is in the same vicinity. But the real event isn't the rate decision. It's the dot plot, the press conference, and whether Warsh removes the Fed's easing bias from the statement. Four FOMC members dissented at the April meeting — the most divided the committee has been since 1992. Warsh inherits a fractured institution.

Ed Yardeni's warning hangs over the June meeting: if the Fed doesn't signal a hawkish pivot, bond markets will decide the Fed is "behind the curve" on inflation and demand a higher risk premium — effectively doing the tightening themselves. BNP Paribas has flagged a rate hike as a genuine tail risk in June, even while calling the probability extremely low. The June 16–17 meeting is the most consequential FOMC in years not because of what they'll do, but because of what Warsh will signal.

Health, Wellness & Fitness

Fat Type Matters for Cancer; Thymus as a Longevity Organ; Melatonin for Night Shift Repair

Cancer Research

Pancreatic Cancer: It's the Type of Fat, Not the Amount

A new study published this week found that when it comes to pancreatic cancer, the kind of dietary fat consumed may matter more than total fat intake. Researchers found one type of fat promoted tumor growth while another cut disease incidence in half. The findings challenge the conventional "eat less fat" narrative and point toward qualitative dietary guidance — specifically, fat source — as a primary variable in cancer risk. Olive oil and omega-3-rich fats are emerging as the protective categories.

Longevity

Your Thymus May Predict How Long You Live

A Mass General Brigham study using AI to analyze CT scans from tens of thousands of adults found that people with healthier thymus glands — the small, often-overlooked immune organ behind the sternum — had significantly better cancer survival outcomes and markers of healthy aging. The thymus shrinks dramatically after puberty in most people, but its residual function at midlife and beyond appears to be a meaningful longevity signal. Lifestyle interventions that preserve thymus function are now an active area of research.

Fitness Science

Melatonin Boosts DNA Repair in Night Shift Workers

New research suggests that melatonin supplements may help night shift workers strengthen the body's DNA repair processes, potentially offsetting some of the cellular damage associated with disrupted circadian rhythms. Night shift work has long been linked to elevated cancer and metabolic disease risk; this finding suggests a low-cost, accessible intervention may partially mitigate that risk. The study adds to growing evidence that melatonin functions beyond its sleep-regulation role.

Editor's Dispatch

Wednesday was the market's first real exhale in weeks — records had been set nine sessions in a row, and the string simply snapped. What's notable is that even with a Broadcom earnings miss, a drone attack on a Gulf state, and a Nasdaq futures selloff, the S&P 500 is still within sight of 7,600. The bull has been battered but not broken. The bigger tests come in sequence: the June 6 jobs report, the June 16–17 FOMC, and whatever the next chapter of the Iran ceasefire looks like. The thing about geopolitical risk premiums is that they deflate as fast as they inflate. One credible diplomatic signal and oil drops $8 in a day. The flip side is also true. Stay anchored to the fundamentals — and watch the bond market. The 30-year Treasury just hit 5.00%. That's the number that matters most.

Random Fact of the Day

The Windy City Isn't That Windy

Chicago's nickname "Windy City" has nothing to do with the weather. It was coined as political slang by New York Sun editor Charles Dana in 1890 to mock Chicagoans for being full of hot air while lobbying for the 1893 World's Fair. Chicago ranks 12th among major U.S. cities for average wind speed — behind Amarillo, TX and Dodge City, KS. On this fine 71-degree Thursday, the name applies in neither sense.

Word of the Day

Contagion (n.)

In finance: the spread of market disturbances — crashes, panic selling, credit freezes — from one market or institution to another, often through mechanisms that weren't anticipated. Broadcom's miss is being watched as a potential contagion vector for the semiconductor sector, which had been the engine of the AI bull market. If the chip stocks catch a cold, the indices may sneeze for longer than one session.
